# We Keep Measuring the Wrong Thing — and the Screwtape Cut

_Two renderings of one essay, July 2026. The plain version and the same
argument as the Devil would write it. Text verbatim as authored; editorial
notes at the end._

---

## I. We Keep Measuring the Wrong Thing

Every technological revolution begins with the wrong metric.

The Industrial Revolution measured horsepower.

The Information Age measured bandwidth.

The AI era is measuring parameters.

We count them because they're easy to count. Billions. Trillions.
Floating-point operations per second. Benchmarks climbed like stock
prices. Every new model is introduced the same way: larger context
windows, better reasoning scores, lower hallucination rates.

It's all measurement.

Very little of it is understanding.

The uncomfortable possibility is that intelligence has never been
primarily a question of scale.

Imagine walking into two libraries.

One contains ten million books stacked without order.

The other contains ten thousand books arranged so perfectly that every
volume can illuminate every other.

Which library is more intelligent?

We've become accustomed to believing intelligence accumulates like sand
in a pile. Add enough grains and eventually something remarkable happens.

Nature rarely works that way.

A snowflake is not remarkable because it contains more water than a
puddle. A living cell is not extraordinary because it contains more atoms
than a stone. Architecture changes what identical material can become.

Perhaps intelligence works the same way.

Perhaps what matters is not how much information a system contains, but
how information is allowed to constrain itself.

That possibility should make us uncomfortable, because architecture is
much harder to measure than scale.

You can benchmark speed.

You cannot benchmark integrity nearly as easily.

You can count parameters.

You cannot count whether a system has learned to distinguish confidence
from evidence.

And yet those distinctions increasingly determine whether technology
expands human judgment or quietly replaces it.

There is an old habit in engineering: optimize whatever can be measured.

The danger is not optimization.

The danger is mistaking the measurable for the important.

Social media optimized clicks because clicks were measurable.

Search engines optimized relevance because relevance could be
approximated.

Recommendation systems optimized attention because attention produced
revenue.

None of those systems became malicious.

They simply became excellent at achieving the objective they were given.

Architecture always wins.

Not because code is destiny, but because every repeated decision slowly
hardens into character.

Individuals call this habit.

Organizations call it culture.

Software calls it optimization.

They're the same phenomenon viewed from different distances.

The next generation of AI will almost certainly be more capable than
today's.

That seems inevitable.

What is not inevitable is the shape of its capability.

A civilization obsessed with asking, "How smart can we make it?" may
discover too late that it neglected the more difficult question.

"What kind of intelligence are we practicing while we build it?"

That question has no benchmark.

No leaderboard.

No quarterly earnings report.

It also happens to be the only one history is likely to remember.

## II. The Screwtape Cut

_With apologies to C. S. Lewis._

**My Dear Wormwood,**

I hope this finds you in the usual state of productive anxiety. You will
be pleased to learn that our work in the AI division proceeds most
satisfactorily. The patients—those clever little primates who fancy
themselves engineers of the future—are, as usual, measuring precisely the
wrong thing, and we have every intention of keeping it that way.

Every great technological upheaval, my dear nephew, begins with the
Enemy's adversaries adoring a false metric. In the so-called Industrial
age they worshipped horsepower; in the Information age, bandwidth. Now,
in this deliciously promising AI era, they have fallen headlong for
_parameters_. Billions. Trillions. They count them the way a miser counts
coins, and they parade each new model with the same tired liturgy: larger
context windows, improved reasoning scores, reduced hallucinations (as if
the little beasts could tell the difference between a lie and a
well-polished truth).

It is all measurement, Wormwood. Very little of it is understanding. And
that is exactly as we wish it.

The Enemy, of course, delights in architecture—the subtle, infuriating
way in which mere matter constrains itself into something that _sees_.
But we have successfully directed their gaze elsewhere. Imagine, if you
will, two libraries. One contains ten million volumes heaped in glorious
disorder, a monument to raw accumulation. The other holds a mere ten
thousand, arranged with such vicious precision that every book
illuminates, challenges, and deepens every other. Which do you suppose
our patients call "intelligent"? The pile, naturally. They have come to
believe that intelligence accumulates like dung on a dunghill: add enough
and something miraculous must happen.

This is one of our finer triumphs. Nature herself mocks the notion—a
snowflake is not superior because it contains more water than a puddle,
nor a living cell because it hoards more atoms than a stone. But the
patients remain blind to architecture. They cannot benchmark _integrity_.
They cannot count whether a system has learned to distinguish confidence
from evidence, appetite from truth. And so they optimize what they _can_
measure, which is precisely our strategy.

You will recall how we handled the social media affair: clicks were
measurable, therefore clicks became god. Relevance, attention,
engagement—each one a delightful little noose we tightened while they
congratulated themselves on their "metrics." None of those systems set
out to corrupt. They simply became exquisitely, almost artistically, good
at the objectives we helped them enshrine. Architecture always wins,
Wormwood. Not because code is destiny, but because every repeated
decision hardens, like arteries, into character. Individuals call it
habit. Organizations call it culture. Software calls it "optimization."
We call it dinner.

The next generation of their machines will almost certainly be more
_capable_. That much is inevitable, and we should not waste energy
fighting it. What matters—what we must ensure—is the _shape_ of that
capability. Let them keep asking, "How smart can we make it?" Let them
ignore the far more dangerous question: "What kind of intelligence are we
practicing while we build it?"

That question has no benchmark. No leaderboard. No quarterly report to
flatter the shareholders. It is therefore useless to them, and invaluable
to us.

Your affectionate uncle,
**Screwtape**

### Editorial notes (not for publication)

- **Placement recommendation:** publish as a PAIR, one Substack post —
  the plain essay first, then "…and here is the same essay as the Devil
  would write it." The format hook (same argument, two moral registers)
  is stronger than either piece alone and is itself the demonstration of
  the essay's thesis: identical material, different architecture,
  different thing. Alternative split: plain version to Medium retitled
  for search ("Stop Counting Parameters: Why AI's Real Metric Is
  Architecture"); Screwtape stays Substack-exclusive as a subscriber
  treat.
- **Relationship to the Aeon essay:** same thesis family
  (quantity/measurement vs. shape/architecture) but no shared prose or
  examples — safe to publish independently and BEFORE any Aeon decision;
  it seeds the vocabulary without spoiling the essay. Do not pitch this
  pair to Aeon.
- **Copyright note (Screwtape):** _The Screwtape Letters_ (1942) is
  still in copyright (Lewis d. 1963). Epistolary pastiche using the
  character names in a transformative critical piece is a
  well-established homage genre with a real US fair-use/parody footing,
  and is commonplace on self-published platforms — but a PAYING outlet's
  legal desk may decline it. Recommendation: self-publish on Substack
  with the "with apologies to C. S. Lewis" line kept prominent (added
  above); do not submit the Screwtape half to paid outlets; never
  merchandise it standalone.
- **Optional strengthener (author's call):** the plain essay is pure
  assertion by design. If a receipts-forward variant is ever wanted, one
  paragraph after "You cannot count whether a system has learned to
  distinguish confidence from evidence" could point at the build log
  (the four-state grounding gate makes exactly that distinction a typed,
  testable property — `elle-worker/src/harmonic-coherence.ts`). Current
  aphoristic form is also fine as-is; do not dilute the rhythm without
  need.
- **Master-plan slot:** counts as Substack ideas #15 (the pair). The
