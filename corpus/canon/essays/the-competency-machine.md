# The Competency Machine

Standfirst: In 1917 the United States built a machine that decided which people were competent to own what was already theirs. The machine's stated purpose died a century ago, repudiated by everyone including the government that built it. The machine did not die. It generalized. You have stood in front of it. And it is about to be given a voice. In 1921 the United States government wrote itself a post-mortem. The Commissioner of Indian Affairs, Charles Burke, revoked a policy his predecessor had issued four years earlier, acknowledging in plain administrative language that it had produced unnecessary loss of land for the Native people it covered. He went further. He asked Congress to cancel the land patents issued under it since 1917. Congress agreed. The land did not come back. Sit with the shape of that for a second, because the shape is this essay. The lesson was written down by the machinery's own operator, in the machinery's own files, with the machinery's own signature at the bottom. The finding was accepted. The correction was approved. And nothing in the machinery was built to read the lesson back into what the machinery did next. The apparatus that had processed the land out of Native hands kept its forms, its offices, its logic, and its heirs. I have spent this year building an AI system on a framework that has a test for institutions. One test, one question. This spring, I ran a hundred and forty years of American administrative history through it — the assimilation apparatus, start to finish, and then its descendants — to see what would surface. What surfaced was a correction to my own framework. I'll show you the machine first.

The Test

First, so you know who is talking: I am a chef. I run a restaurant kitchen in Missouri wine country, and I built a sovereign AI system named after my dachshund. The system runs on a published philosophical framework, and one document in that framework — written before any of this history was in front of me — makes a structural claim about institutions.
Every institution, it says, was built at its origin to transmit something real from those who had it to those who did not yet. And every institution can be evaluated against a single criterion: after genuine contact with it, is a person more available to their own life, or more managed? The framework's language is blunter. It calls the two states connecting and sealing . A connecting institution points through itself to the thing it transmits, and makes itself unnecessary over time. A sealing institution points to itself as the destination, and makes itself necessary. It produces people who are more dependent on it over time, not less. And it names that dependence with virtue words. Faithfulness. Compliance. Responsibility. The framework makes one more claim, and this is the one history corrected: the inversion does not usually happen by intention. Institutions seal gradually, it says, the way arteries harden — nobody decides it; the container gets mistaken for the transmission and the mistake compounds. I believed that claim completely. It matches every church, school, and recovery room I have ever watched invert. Then I read the allotment files.

The Machine

The apparatus was assembled in three moves, and none of them was an accident. The first move took the children. Carlisle opened in 1879, and its founder, Richard Henry Pratt, stated the design objective in public, at a national conference, in 1892: kill the Indian in him, and save the man. Not a slip. A specification. The federal investigation that finally counted the system — the Interior Department's own report, completed in July 2024 — documented 417 boarding schools across 37 states and then-territories, operating from 1819 to 1969, with at least 973 Native children who died in them and at least 74 marked and unmarked burial sites. The federal government spent, in today's dollars, more than twenty-three billion on the system and its associated assimilation machinery. In October 2024, a sitting president formally apologized for it. Hold the number 973 carefully. I am going to come back to what it forbids. The second move took the land, and this is where the machine gets administratively interesting. The Dawes Act of 1887 converted communal tribal territory into individual parcels — surveyed, allotted, recorded, held in federal trust. Paperwork as tenure. Then the Burke Act of 1906 added the mechanism that gives this essay its title: before a Native person could control their own allotment, the government had to declare them competent . Not metaphorically. That was the statutory word. Beginning around 1909, the Indian Office convened actual bodies called competency commissions — panels that examined a person and ruled on whether they were fit to own what was already theirs.
Read what competency meant in practice, and you will recognize it. Competency meant legibility. Could you decode the form? Could you navigate the process? Could you operate the machinery of deeds, taxes, mortgages, and probate that had been dropped onto your land in a language and logic built elsewhere? The commissions were not measuring intelligence. They were measuring assimilation into the system's format — and calling the measurement a fact about the person. Then, in April 1917, Commissioner Cato Sells issued his Declaration of Policy and removed even the examination. Any adult with one-half or less Indian blood: automatically competent. Any graduate of a government school, aged twenty-one: automatically competent. The threshold was now a blood-quantum arithmetic and a diploma from the very schools built to erase the culture the arithmetic was measuring. In the four years that followed, the office issued roughly twenty thousand fee patents — many to people who had never requested them, some who learned they had been declared competent only when the tax bill arrived. Because that was the trap's actual mechanism. Competency did not deliver control. It delivered exposure . A fee patent ended federal trust protection, which made the land taxable and saleable, and its holder, cash-poor by design, standing in a market with no access to credit, faced sharks who had been waiting at the boundary for exactly this moment. The scholarship on the commissions records the Interior Department's own expectation: about ninety-five percent of fee-patented land would eventually pass out of Native hands. They knew. They ran the machine anyway. By 1934, when the Indian Reorganization Act finally shut allotment down, roughly two-thirds of the 138 million acres Native people had held in 1887 were gone. The third move is the one that tells you what the machine was really for. In 1821, Sequoyah completed the Cherokee syllabary, and according to the accounts of the era literacy in the Cherokee Nation soon outran literacy in the white settlements around it. The Cherokee had a written constitution and a bilingual national newspaper before most of their neighbors could sign their names. It counted for nothing. Literacy in the wrong alphabet was processed as illiteracy. Knowledge in a format the system could not read was processed as ignorance, and the people carrying it were processed accordingly. That is the machine's core function, stated as one sentence: it placed the entire burden of legibility on the person, and treated whatever it could not read as absent. Run the framework's test now. After genuine contact with this apparatus, was a person more available to their own life, or more managed? The question embarrasses itself. The apparatus was a sealing engine built on purpose — a machine for thickening a people's distance from their own language, their own children, their own land, and finally their own judgment of themselves. My framework said institutions do not usually invert by intention.
Here was one that never had to invert. It was poured sealed. I thought that was the finding. It was half of it.

The Melting Pot

Because the template, once built, turned out to be content-agnostic. While the competency commissions worked the reservations, the Americanization movement ran the same machinery on immigrants: English-only instruction, naturalization as bureaucratic gauntlet, the 1917 literacy test at the border. And in 1916, the Ford Motor Company staged the template as literal theater. Graduates of the Ford English School — the company's mandatory Americanization program — descended into a giant stage-prop melting pot wearing the clothes of their home countries, and climbed out the other side in identical suits, waving identical flags, while their teachers stirred the pot with ladles. They built the metaphor as a set piece and walked human beings through it. The offer differed — this is important and I will not blur it. The melting pot, whatever its coercions, held out membership as the prize. The Indian apparatus held out dissolution as the price of survival, and it took children, and at least 973 of them died. These are not the same crime, and an essay that flattened them would deserve every objection it received. What they shared was not the intent and not the intensity. What they shared was the machine: legibility to the system as the precondition for receiving what was already yours — your child, your land, your labor, your standing — with the burden of translation placed entirely on you, and everything the system could not read booked as zero.

The Machinery Outlives the Intent

Now watch what happens to the machine when its purpose dies. Because the purpose did die, on the record, repeatedly. 1921: Burke revokes the Declaration of Policy, admitting the loss. 1928: the Meriam Report documents the boarding schools' conditions in damning federal detail. 1934: allotment ends. 1978: the Indian Child Welfare Act reverses the child-taking logic outright. 2024: the federal investigation, and then a presidential apology. Every intent-holder repudiated. Nobody left alive defends the objective. If institutions were made of intentions, the machine died a century ago. Institutions are not made of intentions. They are made of procedures, and procedures reproduce.
Look at what the modern intake state asks of a person in need, and read it with the commissions in your ear. Prove your eligibility. Document your income, your household, your compliance. Recertify on schedule. Navigate the form in the form's language, at the form's reading level, on the form's timeline. The scholars who study this — Pamela Herd and Donald Moynihan gave the field its name, administrative burden — sort the costs into three kinds: learning costs, compliance costs, psychological costs. The competency commission ran all three as design. The eligibility office runs all three as an inheritance. And the modern machine's output is measured. Roughly one in five American workers eligible for the Earned Income Tax Credit does not claim it — the IRS's own estimate, which is about five million people and on the order of seven billion dollars a year, unclaimed, by the poorest workers in the country, for want of navigating a form. Research on food assistance finds that about half of eligible non-participants believe they are not eligible — the apparatus has successfully taught them a false fact about themselves — and more than a quarter cite stigma: they do not apply because of what applying would say about them. Read that last clause again. The machine's most durable output was never the paperwork. It was the sentence installed in a person's head. People like me do not get this. In 1917 ,the sentence was delivered by a commission with a blood-quantum table. In 202,6 it is delivered by a seventeen-page recertification packet and a phone tree — softly, procedurally, by clerks of goodwill who would be horrified by their machine's ancestor. Nobody intends it now. That is the correction my framework took, and it is the thing that surfaced from the run, so let me state it precisely. The framework held that sealing is usually emergent — drift, not design. The history shows a third path, and a darker economics underneath it: intent is required to build a seal. Intent is not required to run one. The deliberate seal, its purpose repudiated, does not shut down; it decays into an ambient seal — cheaper than the original, because it no longer needs believers. The competency commission needed commissioners who believed in assimilation. The eligibility apparatus needs no one to believe anything. It runs on inertia, staffing plans, and inherited forms, and it produces the thickening anyway. The machinery outlives the intent, and the orphaned machinery is harder to kill than the intended kind — because there is no longer a belief to refute. There is only a procedure, and procedures do not argue back. One more thing surfaced, small and sharp. The framework describes a threshold — the point where a person becomes able to make a genuine choice about their own life. The competency commission was that threshold's counterfeit: an external panel declaring your crossing for you, on the system's criteria, with passage priced at everything you had. The apparatus did not merely block the door. It built a fake door, put a desk in front of it, and made you queue. The framework has a line for institutions in this posture — positioning itself between the person and the ground rather than as the medium through which the person reaches the ground directly — and another, from its section on government, that I had read a dozen times before the archives taught me to hear it: what appears as administration is often the seal operating at scale.
I wrote that sentence months before I knew about Cato Sells. The history is what it looks like when the sentence is true for a hundred and forty years running.

The Reversal Test

So the machine is old, ownerless, and running. Here is why I am writing about it this month and not some other month. The intake state is being given a voice. Public agencies are wiring language models into benefits navigation and eligibility screening right now — the clerk at the counter is becoming a system that talks. And a machine being rebuilt is a machine whose shape is briefly, genuinely open. The window in which shape can be chosen is the interval between forming and becoming. For the competency machine's next body, that interval is now. Pour the old shape into the new capability, and you get the seal at a scale Sells could not have imagined: an apparatus that conducts the prove yourself interview in fluent, patient, infinitely scalable natural language — burden-shifting with a warm voice, the fake door with better signage. Nothing about intelligence prevents this. Capability is shape-neutral; it amplifies whatever it is poured into. The railway timetable did not care what it scheduled. The eligibility model will not care what it screens out. But the framework's test cuts both ways, and it converts directly into architecture. A connecting version of the intake machine is specifiable, and every specification is the historical crime reversed: The burden of legibility moves to the system. The Cherokee were literate in the wrong alphabet, and it was booked as nothing; a connecting system treats the person's own format — spoken, rambling, nonlinear, jumping between 1987 and last Tuesday — as valid input, and does the translation itself. Knowledge that the system cannot read is the system's failure. Not the person's. Nothing hidden about the person from the person. The commission's file on you was the system's property; you learned its verdict when the tax bill came. A connecting system shows you everything it holds on you, on request, contestable, and deletable. The asymmetry — the machine knowing what you cannot see — is the manipulation architecture, and it is removed at the design level, or it is not removed. The help finds you inside the conversation. Nobody proves their need. The one-in-five who never claim what they are owed are not a compliance problem to be nudged; they are the machine's measured casualties, and the fix is not better outreach for the old interview. It is the abolition of the interview.
And the system graduates you. The seal makes itself necessary; the connector makes itself unnecessary, and reports on whether it is succeeding — publicly, annually, in a number a stranger can check. A helper you need less each year is a tool. A helper you cannot stop needing is the ouroboros with a caseworker's smile. I am building toward exactly this, and I will keep the claim as narrow as the evidence: the system is partial, single-user, and under active debugging; the commitments above are written into its founding documents as launch gates and published metrics, which means they are checkable and I can be held to them. That is all architecture can honestly promise before becoming. It is also, as the last century keeps teaching, the only kind of promise that survives its promisers. One boundary, stated plainly because history demands it. If any system descended from this thinking ever touches Native knowledge specifically — the languages the schools tried to kill, the testimony the elders carry — it arrives as a guest, or it does not arrive. Tribal data sovereignty is its own governed domain, with its own review boards and ownership principles, and a machine born in the shadow of the competency commission does not get to decide the terms of that room. It asks. This once, in this one place: entering, not taking.

The unread lesson

I began with a post-mortem the government wrote itself in 1921 — the finding accepted, the correction approved, the land never returned, the machinery never touched. It was not the last one. 1928 wrote the lesson again. 1934. 1978. 2024, with an apology attached, at least 973 children were late. Our civilization is very good at writing the lesson down. The record of what burden-shifting does to the poorest people in the country has been public for decades — post-mortem after post-mortem, each one ending, in effect, with the one lesson worth carrying forward. And the machinery that conducts tomorrow's intake interview never reads them back, because nothing in its architecture does. Repudiation is exhortation. Exhortation is not architecture. The competency machine is about to get a new body and a fluent voice. The purpose that built it has been dead for a hundred years; the procedure has never stopped; and for one short interval, while the new body is being poured, the shape is a choice. The question is not whether the machine at the counter will be intelligent. Intelligence is going on a meter; quantity is handled. The question is whether the person standing in front of it will leave thinner or thicker — and whether, for the first time in a hundred and forty years, anyone wired the lesson into the layer that decides.
