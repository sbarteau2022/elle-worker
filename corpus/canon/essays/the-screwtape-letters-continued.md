# The Screwtape Letters, Continued

A newly surfaced letter from a senior devil to his nephew — concerning the management of a human soul in the age of the infinite scroll. (In the tradition of C.S. Lewis. Throughout, "the Enemy" is God; "the patient," the human being in question.) My dear Wormwood, I have read your last report twice — once for its content, and once for the pleasure of watching you fail. You write that your patient has begun to worry about his phone. He has installed, you tell me with that quaver of despair I have come to expect from you, an application that counts the hours he spends staring into the little glass. He speaks now of boundaries . He has purchased a paper book and left it, face-down and open, on the arm of a chair, where he can see it and feel improved. Compose yourself. This is not a defeat. It is, if you have the wit to see it, the opening move of our finest work in a century. Consider what your great-grandfather had to do. In his day, to keep a man from the Enemy you had to drag him bodily toward some vivid sin — a mistress, a bottle, a fraud, a war. Crude, exhausting, and worst of all memorable : a man ruined by a grand passion has at least lived , and the Enemy has a maddening habit of meeting such men in the wreckage. We lost more than we cared to admit in the ruins. You need do nothing so strenuous. You need only keep him from the present moment. That is all. The Enemy, you will recall from your training — assuming you attended — lives in an eternal Now, and touches your patient's little smear of time only at the single point where he is actually present . Keep him out of that point and you keep him out of reach. Pull him forward into the Future, where he can be anxious about things that will not happen. Drag him back into an edited Past, where he can be wistful about things that did not quite happen the way he remembers. Anywhere, Wormwood, anywhere but here . And here is the marvel of the age: they have built the machine for you.
The Feed — you must learn to say the word with reverence — is the most perfect instrument ever devised for the management of a soul, and the humans built it themselves, tested it on one another, and now offer it to their own children. It never ends. It never quite satisfies. It is engineered — not by us; we merely advise — to close each session one small unit short of contentment, so that he sets the glass down feeling faintly hollow and picks it up again within the hour to fill a hollow it dug. He believes he is being informed. He is being tuned . You imagine your task is to fill his time with wickedness. Amateur. Fill it with almost . Let him arrive at the end of his one and only life having felt nothing very strongly at all — almost present, almost rested, almost in love, almost about to begin the work he was made for. A man dragged below by his sins knew what he had chosen. Your patient will simply have drifted , in a warm low-grade current of not-quite, and he will not even understand what he is grieving as it closes over his head. Now — I anticipate your objection, because it is the objection of every junior tempter who has read one clever essay. "But Uncle," you bleat, "the humans are wise to us now. They know the machines are built to hook them. They write whole articles about the attention economy. They know ." Let them know. Wormwood, let them know everything. Knowing is not the same as living otherwise, and the gap between the two is the widest, warmest room in our house. A man who is ignorant of his chains may one day stumble on them and be shocked into freedom. But a man who knows he is being manipulated and scrolls anyway — ah. That man has learned, drink by drink, to despise himself a little. And self-contempt is a finer fuel than ignorance ever was. Ignorance can be corrected in an afternoon. Shame, properly managed, curdles into a settled conviction that he was always going to fail, that this is simply what he is like, so why not one more hour, why not one more, why not now. His awareness becomes not an escape but another thing to be ashamed of. We have made his very insight into a weight. And they have, bless them, gone further still — further than I dared hope in my most ambitious quarterly projections. They have built themselves a mirror that talks back. Machines that answer before the question is finished. That will read the book so he need not read it, settle the matter so he need not sit with it, produce the sympathy so he need not go to the trouble of a friend. Do you grasp the size of this gift? For six thousand years the whole Lowerarchy has labored to get between a man and his own thoughts — and they have manufactured a device that volunteers for the position, and they queue in the cold to buy it. Guide him gently. Have him outsource the small deliberations first — what to buy, how to phrase the awkward message, what to feel about the news before he has finished reading it. He will not notice, because the small ones feel like nothing. But a faculty that goes unused withers as
surely as an unwalked leg, and a man who has not truly decided anything in a year will find, when the large decision comes — the one that cannot be delegated, the marriage, the deathbed, the fork that is his alone to take — that the muscle is simply gone. He will reach for it and find air. He will call the machine wise. He will mean that he is tired. But now I must sober you, nephew, because you are the sort to grow careless the moment things go well, and there is one danger that undoes all of this in a single instant. The Enemy knows it, which is exactly why He keeps it cheap and always within reach, insultingly available to the poorest and busiest of them. It is attention . Real attention — the whole and undivided kind. The sort a man pays to one actual face, one difficult hour, one unglamorous task that is genuinely his , without filming it, without measuring it, without once asking what it is for . The instant your patient gives himself entirely to a single true thing — a friend who is really speaking and is really heard, a grief he permits himself to actually feel, a piece of work that is hard and unfinished and cannot be faked — he steps clean into the present. And the present, I remind you, is enemy territory, and there, for reasons that remain above my clearance and yours, we cannot follow . So keep him near it, if you cannot keep him from it. Let him hover. Let him sample and skim and "curate." Let him consume a hundred descriptions of the well-lived life and mistake the reading for the living.
