# CAPTURED RESONANCE ACROSS SCALES

A General Structural Account of Biological Failure, with Cellular, Neurodegenerative, and Behavioral Instantiations

# Abstract

This paper proposes that a single structural pathology — captured resonance — recurs across multiple scales of biological organization, with consistent defining features and a consistent recovery geometry at each scale. The argument is supported by three instantiations: cancer at the cellular and tissue scale, with the Soto-Sonnenschein tissue organization field theory and the Mossio-Montévil closure-of-constraints framework as the relevant theoretical grounds; Alzheimer's disease at the neurodegenerative scale, with the receiver model and amplitude-depletion principle developed elsewhere in the corpus as the relevant structural account; and addiction at the behavioral and cognitive scale, with the convergent evidence from environmental modulation studies, prefrontal cortex neuroimaging, habit-to-compulsion transitions, the shame spiral, and active inference, unified in the companion paper Captured Resonance (Barteau, 2026a). The cross-scale claim is that these are not three diseases related by analogy but three instantiations of one structural form. The structural form is specified formally in terms of nested Markov blankets and free energy minimization under suboptimal models, drawing on the Markov-blankets-of-life framework developed by Kirchhoff, Parr, Palacios, Friston & Kiverstein (2018), extended into philosophy of mind by Kirchhoff & Kiverstein (2021), together with the foundational free energy principle work of Friston (2010, 2013) and its developmental extension in Friston, Levin, Sengupta & Pezzulo (2015). The paper specifies what captured resonance adds to the active inference and closure-of-constraints programs, identifies three falsifiable empirical predictions across the scales examined, and develops the unified recovery geometry that follows from the structural account.

# 1. The Problem the Field Has Not Yet Posed

Contemporary biology and biomedicine have produced extraordinarily rich scale-specific accounts of pathology. The somatic mutation theory of cancer, the amyloid-tau cascade of Alzheimer's disease, the brain-disease and habit-formation theories of addiction — each is internally coherent, empirically grounded, and clinically productive. Each is also, by design, limited to its own scale of organization. The cellular biologist does not look for behavioral instantiations of the structural form she studies. The neurodegeneration researcher does not look for cellular instantiations of the network dynamics he characterizes. The addiction researcher does not look for cellular instantiations of the captured attentional pattern she documents.

This separation is methodologically defensible. The empirical work at each scale is hard enough that specializing is required. The risk, however, is that real cross-scale structural identities can fail to be noticed because no research program is looking for them. The argument of this paper is that one such structural identity has now accumulated sufficient empirical anchors across three scales that it can be named and tested as a unified phenomenon: a structural pathology that recurs, with consistent defining features, at the cellular, neurodegenerative, and behavioral scales of biological organization, with a consistent recovery geometry at each scale.

The pathology has a name in this paper. The name is captured resonance, introduced in the companion paper on addiction (Barteau, 2026a) and developed at length there. Captured resonance is the structural form a biological system enters when a stable attractor against the substrate's maintenance recruits the substrate's own integrative resources into the attractor's maintenance, dependent on surrounding conditions that suppress alternatives. The companion paper develops this account for the behavioral scale, unifying five empirical literatures on addiction under a single structural mechanism. This paper extends the account across scales, examines the cellular and neurodegenerative instantiations, and proposes the unified recovery geometry.

The contribution is not the empirical work at any one scale. The empirical work at each scale has been done by others, in programs that this paper engages with respect and at depth. The contribution is the structural identification: that what cancer biology has been documenting at the cellular scale, what neurodegeneration research has been documenting at the network scale, and what addiction science has been documenting at the behavioral scale are not three pathologies linked by analogy. They are three instantiations of one structural form.

# 2. Captured Resonance: The General Structural Form

The general structural form of captured resonance is specified by three defining features, present together. These features are scale-invariant: they appear at every scale at which captured resonance occurs, in scale-specific empirical guises.

Feature one: a stable attractor against substrate maintenance. The system has entered a configuration that is consuming the conditions of its own continuation. The configuration is not merely costly or maladaptive in the loose sense; it is phase-locked in such a way that the system returns to it under perturbation. The substrate maintaining the system degrades as the attractor stabilizes. This feature is what makes captured resonance distinct from ordinary system stress, in which substrate degradation triggers corrective responses that pull the system back toward homeostasis.

Feature two: recruitment of the substrate's integrative resources into the attractor's maintenance. The faculty that should operate correctively against the attractor has instead been incorporated into the attractor's substrate. At the cellular scale, this is the recruitment of regulatory mechanisms — apoptotic signaling, immune surveillance, metabolic control — into the maintenance of the malignant phenotype. At the network scale, this is the recruitment of recognition and resonance operations into patterns that degrade the network's capacity to maintain coherent function. At the behavioral and cognitive scale, this is the recruitment of awareness, self-monitoring, and recognition itself into the maintenance of the addictive pattern. The general form: the integrative faculty is no longer external to the attractor; it has become part of what the attractor uses to stabilize.

Feature three: dependence on suppression of alternatives in the surrounding environment. The attractor's stability is not intrinsic to the system; it depends on the surrounding environment being configured such that corrective alternatives are unavailable, suppressed, or rendered inaccessible. Remove the surrounding suppression and the attractor's stability is reduced. Restore the suppression and the attractor returns. At the cellular scale, this is the tumor microenvironment. At the network scale, this is the reduced cognitive reserve and reduced environmental enrichment associated with neurodegenerative progression. At the behavioral scale, this is the environmental configuration that suppresses the addict's access to corrective relationships, structures, and alternatives — the finding that Alexander's Rat Park program documented at the behavioral scale (Alexander, Coambs & Hadaway, 1978) and that has been replicated conceptually across the broader addiction literature.

Where these three features are present together, the system is in captured resonance. Where one or more is absent, the system may be in a related but structurally distinct pathology — homeostatic failure, repair failure, adaptive inflexibility — that does not have the same recovery geometry and does not respond to the same interventions. The distinction matters clinically and theoretically. Treatments designed for captured resonance will not help conditions that lack the structural form, and may harm them. Treatments designed for ordinary homeostatic failure will not help captured resonance, and may deepen it.

# 3. The Formal Account: Nested Markov Blankets and the Pathology of Recruited Closure

The general structural form of captured resonance admits a formal characterization that draws on two converging theoretical programs: the active inference and free energy principle framework developed by Friston and colleagues (Friston, 2010, 2013), and the Markov-blankets-of-life extension of that framework developed by Kirchhoff, Parr, Palacios, Friston, and Kiverstein (2018), with its philosophy-of-mind elaboration in Kirchhoff and Kiverstein (2021). The closure-of-constraints framework developed by Montévil, Mossio, Longo, and Soto (Montévil & Mossio, 2015) provides a complementary formalism at the biological-organization level. Together, these programs supply the mathematical and conceptual grounding the cross-scale claim requires.

The free energy principle characterizes a biological system as a partition of states with a Markov blanket separating internal from external states, mediated by sensory and active states. Internal states act on the environment through active states to bring sensory inputs into alignment with the system's internal model; the internal model updates in response to prediction errors that exceed expected precision. The system maintains itself by minimizing variational free energy at the blanket. Friston's 2013 paper establishes that any ergodic random dynamical system possessing a Markov blanket will appear to actively maintain its structural and dynamical integrity, which is the formal definition of biological self-organization the framework offers.

Kirchhoff and colleagues' 2018 paper extends this formalism in a way that is load-bearing for the cross-scale claim of this paper. The extension makes two structural moves. First, it establishes that any living system — from individual cells to complex multicellular organisms — possesses a Markov blanket, and that this provides a formal, scale-invariant definition of biological boundary. Second, and more consequentially, it demonstrates that Markov blankets can nest. A collective of Markov-blanketed entities can self-assemble into a global system that itself possesses a Markov blanket, with internal-external partitioning at every level of the nesting. Living systems are characterized by layered, nested Markov blankets that are mutually constitutive: cells assemble into tissues, tissues into organs, organs into organisms, organisms into ecosystems, with each level realizing its own statistical boundary while participating in the boundary structure of the level above.

This nested Markov blanket framework is the formal grounding the captured-resonance argument requires. Without it, the cross-scale claim would be analogical at best. With it, the cross-scale claim becomes the direct consequence of a single structural feature operating at multiple levels of the same nested architecture: captured resonance is a pathology of nested Markov blankets, characterized by specific dysfunctional dynamics at the blanket level, and capable of being realized at any scale at which the nested-blanket structure exists.

A third move in the Kirchhoff et al. 2018 paper is critical for understanding the third defining feature of captured resonance. The authors demonstrate that the Markov blanket of a living system need not be co-extensive with its biophysical boundary. The blanket is a statistical structure defined by conditional independence relations in the system's dynamics, not a membrane or a skin. Where the dynamics extend into the surrounding environment, so does the blanket. A cell's Markov blanket includes aspects of its microenvironment that are dynamically coupled to its internal states. An organism's Markov blanket includes aspects of its ecological niche that are dynamically coupled to its physiology. A cognitive agent's Markov blanket, as Kirchhoff and Kiverstein argue in their 2021 paper, can extend into the environment in ways that the extended mind tradition has long argued for but that the Markov blanket formalism now allows to be specified precisely.

This is the formal grounding for what captured resonance's third defining feature describes. The environmental dependence of the captured attractor is not an external condition that the pathology happens to have. It is a property of the system's own Markov blanket, which has come to include environmental elements that participate in the maintenance of the pathological attractor. The tumor microenvironment is part of the captured cellular system's Markov blanket. The environmental conditions that suppress alternatives in addiction are part of the captured behavioral system's Markov blanket. The cognitive isolation that accelerates neurodegenerative progression is part of the captured network system's Markov blanket. In each case, the blanket has extended into the environment in ways that recruit the environment into the pathology rather than allowing the environment to function correctively against it.

With this formalism in place, captured resonance can be specified precisely. The system's internal states have incorporated a prior with high precision-weighting that does not align with substrate maintenance — a prior the active inference literature on addiction (Schwartenbeck et al., 2015; Miller, Kiverstein & Rietveld, 2020) characterizes as a suboptimal generative model with pathologically narrowed priors. The system's active states are deployed to suppress prediction errors that would challenge the pathological prior, including by reshaping the sensory states the blanket admits and by reshaping the environmental conditions to which the system is exposed. The Markov blanket, which under healthy conditions mediates the system's open exchange with its environment, becomes a mechanism for closing the system around its own pathology. The closure is real, in Kirchhoff's sense — it is a genuine topological feature of the system's dynamics, not an artifact of description. What has changed is not the existence of the closure but its function. Healthy closure realizes the system's organization. Captured closure realizes the system's degradation.

The closure-of-constraints framework provides a parallel and complementary formalism at the level of biological organization. Montévil and Mossio (2015) characterize biological organization as a network of mutually dependent constraints — entities that act upon thermodynamic processes while exhibiting conservation at relevant time scales, and that are themselves maintained by other constraints in the network. Healthy biological organization is the realization of this closure. Captured resonance is the specific structural form in which the closure-of-constraints network has reorganized around a pathological attractor: the constraints that should be correcting the attractor have been recruited into its maintenance, and the network of mutual dependencies that should be realizing the system's healthy organization is now realizing the attractor's stability. The closure persists. What has changed is what the closure is closing around.

The two formalisms — nested Markov blankets and closure of constraints — describe the same structural phenomenon from different vantages. The Markov blanket framework describes the topological boundary conditions and dynamical state of the system. The closure-of-constraints framework describes the network of biological constraints that realize the system's organization. In captured resonance, both formalisms identify the same pathology: the topology has sealed around an attractor that should have been corrected, and the constraint network has reorganized to maintain the attractor it should have been disrupting. Each formalism predicts in principle that such a pathology is possible. Neither, until this paper, has characterized it as a unified failure mode operating at multiple scales of the nested biological hierarchy.

This is the formal account of captured resonance. The remainder of this paper develops the three biological instantiations the framework identifies, the cross-scale recognition operation that captured resonance disrupts, the falsifiable predictions the framework generates, and the unified recovery geometry that follows from the structural account.

# 4. The Cellular Instantiation: Cancer

Cancer presents the three defining features of captured resonance at the cellular and tissue scales, with mechanistic specifics that distinguish the captured-resonance reading from the dominant somatic mutation theory and that align with the alternative tissue organization field theory of carcinogenesis.

The dominant theoretical framework in cancer biology since the mid-twentieth century has been the somatic mutation theory, which holds that cancer is a clonal, cell-based disease driven by the accumulation of mutations that confer proliferative and invasive advantages on individual cells. The theory has been productive at the level of identifying specific oncogenes, tumor suppressors, and molecular targets for therapy. It has also encountered persistent difficulties: the failure of mutation burden to predict clinical behavior in many tumor types, the existence of cancers with no identifiable driver mutations, the regression of advanced tumors under conditions that do not target the malignant cells themselves, and the resurgence of malignant phenotypes from cells that lack the originating mutations. Soto and Sonnenschein (2011), drawing on a long tradition in organicist biology, proposed an alternative: the tissue organization field theory, which holds that cancer is a tissue-based disease in which proliferation is the default state of cells, and that carcinogenesis is a failure of the tissue-level organization that normally constrains cellular proliferation rather than a gain of function in individual cells.

The tissue organization field theory is the cellular-scale instantiation of captured resonance, when read alongside the closure-of-constraints framework that grounds it formally. The first defining feature, a stable attractor against substrate maintenance, is what the tissue organization field theory characterizes as the malignant phenotype: not a property of individual cells but a property of the tissue-level dynamics those cells participate in. The attractor is the dysregulated tissue organization, and the attractor is stable in the technical sense — therapeutic interventions that reduce tumor burden are followed, in most cases, by recurrence, because the underlying tissue-level attractor has not been disrupted. The cells return to the attractor configuration even when many of the original cells have been eliminated. The attractor is not the cells; it is the dynamics the cells are constrained into.

The second defining feature, recruitment of the substrate's integrative resources into the attractor's maintenance, is documented across the cancer biology literature in ways the captured-resonance framework unifies as a single structural phenomenon. The malignant attractor is maintained by the systematic recruitment of regulatory mechanisms that should be correcting it. Programmed cell death, the mechanism by which transformed cells are normally eliminated, is hijacked: malignant cells acquire mutations and epigenetic modifications that allow them to evade apoptotic signaling, and the surrounding tissue is reorganized to reduce the apoptotic pressure on the malignant population. Immune surveillance, the mechanism by which the immune system recognizes and eliminates transformed cells, is evaded through active immunosuppression: tumors recruit regulatory T cells, induce immune checkpoint expression, and reshape the local immune environment to disable rather than support antitumor responses. Metabolic regulation, which should constrain cellular proliferation to the tissue's metabolic capacity, is reprogrammed to support the malignant attractor's energetic requirements through aerobic glycolysis, altered amino acid metabolism, and metabolic competition with surrounding non-malignant cells. Each of these is a faculty that, in healthy tissue, operates correctively against transformed cells. In the malignant state, each has been recruited into the attractor's maintenance.

The closure-of-constraints framework provides the formal grounding for this recruitment. Montévil and Mossio (2015) characterize biological organization as a network of mutually dependent constraints, each maintained by others in the network. Healthy tissue organization is the realization of this closure at the tissue scale. The malignant attractor represents a reorganization of the closure: the constraints that should be regulating cellular proliferation, differentiation, and elimination have been reorganized into a network that maintains the malignant phenotype rather than constraining it. This is not absence of organization. The organization persists. What has changed is what the organization is organizing around. The constraint network has not failed; it has been recruited.

The third defining feature, dependence on suppression of alternatives in the surrounding environment, is exemplified by the tumor microenvironment, which has emerged as a central focus of cancer biology over the past two decades. The malignant attractor depends on its surrounding tissue being configured to support rather than challenge it. The tumor microenvironment is characterized by altered stromal interactions, suppressed immune infiltration, dysregulated growth factor signaling, hypoxia, acidosis, and metabolic conditions that disadvantage non-malignant alternatives. Each of these features functions to suppress the alternatives — non-malignant tissue configurations, immune responses, metabolic competitors — that would, under healthy conditions, destabilize the malignant attractor. Read through the Kirchhoff framework, the tumor microenvironment is the extension of the malignant system's Markov blanket into the surrounding tissue, in ways that make the environment a participant in the pathology rather than a corrective against it.

The predictions of this reading are consistent with the empirical record. Interventions that restore the suppressed alternatives — immunotherapy that re-engages immune surveillance, microenvironmental modifications that restore normal tissue architecture, interventions that disrupt the tumor's metabolic privilege — produce structural responses that interventions targeting only the malignant cells do not. Conversely, interventions that successfully eliminate large fractions of the malignant cell population but leave the microenvironmental conditions intact typically produce attenuation followed by reconstitution of the malignant attractor. This is the captured-resonance prediction: removing the suppression destabilizes the attractor; targeting the substrate without removing the suppression allows the attractor to reconstitute from whatever cellular material remains compatible with the still-present microenvironmental conditions.

Captured resonance, at the cellular and tissue scale, is cancer in the sense the tissue organization field theory and the closure-of-constraints framework together characterize. Specifically, it is the structural form that emerges when the Soto-Sonnenschein account of tissue-level organizational failure is read together with the Mossio-Montévil account of constraint-network reorganization, and when the Kirchhoff-extended Markov blanket framework is applied to specify how the malignant system's statistical boundary has come to include the environmental conditions that maintain its stability. The captured-resonance framework names what these three programs have been describing from different vantages as a single structural pathology with three defining features.

# 5. The Neurodegenerative Instantiation: Alzheimer's Disease

Alzheimer's disease presents the three defining features of captured resonance at the neurodegenerative and network scales, with a structural reading that complements rather than replaces the dominant amyloid-tau cascade hypothesis.

The dominant theoretical framework for Alzheimer's disease has been the amyloid cascade hypothesis, which holds that the accumulation of amyloid-beta plaques and tau tangles drives the neurodegenerative progression characteristic of the disease. The hypothesis has guided drug development for several decades and has produced therapeutics that successfully reduce amyloid burden. The clinical outcomes of these therapeutics, however, have been disappointing: amyloid reduction has not consistently produced cognitive improvement or arrested clinical progression, and a substantial fraction of patients with significant amyloid burden show no clinical signs of cognitive impairment. This dissociation between amyloid burden and clinical presentation has motivated reconsideration of what the disease structurally is.

The corpus's Alzheimer's trilogy develops an alternative reading framed around what it calls the receiver model. The brain's capacity to maintain coherent cognitive function depends, on this reading, on its capacity to support resonance with patterns that integrate sensory, mnemonic, and self-referential information across time. Sundowning — the documented worsening of cognitive symptoms in the late afternoon and evening in many Alzheimer's patients — is characterized as amplitude depletion: as the day's cognitive demands accumulate, the system's capacity to support the integrative resonance is progressively reduced, and the network's coherent function degrades along a temporal gradient. The broader progression of Alzheimer's disease is read as the progressive depletion of this resonance capacity. The corpus's trilogy proposes that the depletion is not merely loss of neurons but loss of the resonance capacity that the neuronal substrate supports — the receiver function failing before the receiver hardware is fully destroyed.

Read as an instantiation of captured resonance, the Alzheimer's phenomenon shows the three defining features with mechanistic specifics that the receiver model and amplitude-depletion principle together characterize.

The first feature, a stable attractor against substrate maintenance, is exemplified by the progressive trajectory of the disease. The network does not return to function with rest or intervention; it stabilizes in progressively degraded configurations. Each apparent plateau in the disease's progression is a new attractor, and each transition is the system entering a more deeply captured state. The trajectory is graded but the stability at each stage is real, in the technical sense: the network returns to the degraded configuration under perturbation, and interventions that produce temporary improvements typically do not arrest the underlying progression. The attractor is not the accumulation of pathological proteins per se; it is the dynamical state the network has entered, in which the resonance capacity has been progressively reduced and the system's mode of operation has reorganized around the depletion.

The second feature, recruitment of integrative resources into the attractor's maintenance, is what the receiver model and the amplitude-depletion principle together characterize. The recognition operation — the network's capacity to recognize its own patterns across time, to integrate present input with stored representations, to maintain the resonance that constitutes recognizable cognition — is itself being consumed by the pathological process. The faculty that should be doing the integrating is being recruited into a pattern that degrades the system's capacity to integrate. This is structurally distinct from the standard cascade model, which characterizes Alzheimer's as the accumulation of pathological proteins; the captured-resonance account is compatible with the cascade model as a description of the molecular mechanism but proposes that the cascade is the substrate through which the deeper structural pathology is realized. The pathological proteins are not the disease; the disease is the recruited closure of the recognition operation against the substrate that supports it. The proteins accumulate as a consequence of the recruitment, and reducing the proteins without addressing the recruitment leaves the underlying pathology intact.

The third feature, dependence on environmental suppression of alternatives, is exemplified by the well-documented protective effect of cognitive reserve and environmental enrichment against Alzheimer's progression. Engagement with cognitive challenge, social complexity, structured external scaffolding, and meaningful relational activity slows the disease's progression and, in some cases, attenuates clinical presentation despite continuing neuropathological accumulation. Conversely, social isolation, sensory deprivation, reduction in cognitive demand, and removal of structured external scaffolding accelerate progression. The environmental factors do not eliminate the pathology; they appear to disrupt the captured pattern's stability by providing alternatives that the network can resonate with even as its intrinsic resonance capacity is depleted. Read through the Kirchhoff framework, the network's Markov blanket extends into the cognitive and social environment in ways that make environmental engagement constitutive of the network's healthy function; isolation collapses the blanket back toward the biophysical boundary and removes the environmental scaffolding the resonance capacity depends on.

Captured resonance, at the neurodegenerative and network scale, is Alzheimer's disease in the sense the receiver model and amplitude-depletion principle together characterize. Specifically, it is the structural form that emerges when the network's recognition operation has been recruited into a pattern that depletes the substrate supporting it, when the integrative resources that should be correcting the depletion have been incorporated into its maintenance, and when the environmental scaffolding that would supply external recognition operations has been removed. The captured-resonance reading does not contradict the amyloid-tau cascade hypothesis as a description of molecular mechanism. It proposes that the molecular cascade is the substrate through which a deeper structural pathology — recruited closure of the recognition operation against the substrate that supports it — is realized.

# 6. The Behavioral Instantiation: Addiction

Addiction is developed at length in the companion paper Captured Resonance (Barteau, 2026a). The summary here serves to complete the cross-scale picture and to specify how the addiction case relates to the cellular and neurodegenerative cases.

Addiction presents the three defining features at the behavioral and cognitive scale. The first feature, stable attractor against substrate maintenance, is documented by the habit-to-compulsion transition literature (Everitt & Robbins, 2016): the addicted system has entered a stable mode of operation that returns to itself under perturbation, with neural substrates measurably distinct from goal-directed behavior. The second feature, recruitment of integrative resources into the attractor's maintenance, is documented by the impaired insight literature (Goldstein & Volkow, 2011) and the shame spiral data (Batchelder et al., 2022): awareness, self-monitoring, and self-conscious emotion are recruited into the maintenance of the addictive pattern rather than functioning correctively against it. The third feature, environmental suppression of alternatives, is documented by the Rat Park program (Alexander, Coambs & Hadaway, 1978) and its conceptual replication literature: the addictive attractor depends on environmental conditions that suppress access to alternatives.

The companion paper develops these in detail and proposes the formal account in terms of active inference (Schwartenbeck et al., 2015; Miller, Kiverstein & Rietveld, 2020). What this paper adds to the addiction case is the recognition that the same structural form has been documented at other scales, and that the recovery geometry developed for addiction — the de-recruitment of awareness through external precision sources — has structural counterparts at the cellular and neurodegenerative scales.

# 7. The Cross-Scale Recognition Operation

Across the three scales examined, the same operation is being captured: recognition. At the cellular scale, healthy tissue depends on cells recognizing each other, recognizing their position in the tissue, recognizing signals that maintain differentiation. Captured resonance at this scale is the disruption of cellular recognition — cells failing to recognize position, failing to receive differentiation signals, failing to be recognized by immune surveillance as transformed. At the network and recognition scale, healthy cognition depends on the network recognizing its own patterns across time. Captured resonance at this scale is the disruption of this self-recognition — the receiver function failing, the amplitude depleting, the resonance with integrative patterns being progressively lost. At the behavioral and cognitive scale, healthy behavior depends on the system recognizing itself and recognizing its environment accurately, with that recognition functioning correctively. Captured resonance at this scale is the disruption of this corrective recognition — the awareness recruited into the attractor it should be correcting.

The recognition operation is not a metaphor that happens to apply at three different scales. It is the same formal operation, instantiated at three different scales of biological organization, with the same structural form of failure across scales. The corpus's work on memory (Barteau, 2026b) develops this in detail: memory is not storage but the recognition operation through which a system maintains its continuity with itself across time. Captured resonance is the failure of this operation, occurring when the operation has been recruited against the substrate that supports it. The scale-invariance of the recognition operation, and the scale-invariance of its captured form, is the substantive content of the cross-scale claim this paper makes.

# 8. Predictions and Falsifiable Claims

The captured-resonance framework generates specific empirical predictions across scales. Three are stated here as falsifiable claims that distinguish the unified framework from scale-specific accounts.

Prediction one (cross-scale recurrence pattern). Across all three scales, interventions that successfully reduce the substrate of the pathology without disrupting the recruited closure or the environmental suppression of alternatives will produce a characteristic pattern: initial attenuation followed by reconstitution of the captured attractor on a timescale that reflects the substrate's regeneration capacity. The framework predicts that this pattern will be observed with similar structural properties across scales: tumor recurrence after substrate-directed therapy that leaves the tumor microenvironment intact; Alzheimer's progression continuing despite amyloid reduction in patients whose cognitive scaffolding has not been restored; addiction relapse following detoxification without environmental change. The cross-scale prediction is that the kinetics of reconstitution, when measured against the substrate's regeneration capacity at each scale, should show comparable structural properties. This prediction is falsifiable: if the reconstitution kinetics across scales show no structural commonality when normalized for substrate dynamics, the cross-scale claim is significantly weakened.

Prediction two (external precision source efficacy). The framework predicts that interventions introducing sources of recognition that the captured system cannot recruit into its own attractor will produce structural responses where substrate-directed interventions have failed, and that the efficacy of such interventions will scale with the degree to which the introduced precision source is genuinely external to the captured system's closure. At the cellular scale, this predicts that immunotherapy approaches that successfully re-engage immune recognition will outperform substrate-directed therapies in cancers exhibiting full captured-resonance structure, and that the magnitude of the differential will correlate with measures of the tumor microenvironment's immunosuppressive activity. At the neurodegenerative scale, this predicts that structured external scaffolding supplying recognition operations the network has lost capacity for will produce greater clinical benefit than pharmacological substrate-directed approaches, and that the differential will correlate with measures of the network's residual recognition capacity. At the behavioral scale, the documented efficacy of peer support, therapeutic community, and threshold structures (developed in the companion paper) is consistent with this prediction. The cross-scale prediction is testable: differential efficacy between substrate-directed and external-precision-source interventions should follow comparable patterns across scales when controlled for disease stage.

Prediction three (environmental destabilization). The framework predicts that the captured pattern's stability is environment-dependent in ways the scale-specific frameworks do not fully predict, and that environments configured to supply alternatives will destabilize captured resonance at every scale. The Rat Park finding at the behavioral scale, the cognitive reserve effect at the neurodegenerative scale, and the tumor microenvironment effect at the cellular scale are the same prediction at three scales. The specific cross-scale prediction is that systematic studies of environmental enrichment as an adjunctive intervention should show structural commonalities in dose-response relationships across the three scales when measured against scale-appropriate outcome variables. This prediction is falsifiable: if environmental enrichment produces no benefit at one or more scales when comparable interventions show benefit at others, the cross-scale unification is weakened.

Each prediction is testable independently and the three together constitute a coherent empirical program. The framework's success or failure can be measured against these predictions over time.

# 9. Relation to Existing Frameworks

The captured-resonance framework is positioned in specific relationships to four existing programs. None of these programs is contradicted by the framework; in each case, the framework extends or sharpens an account that the program has developed.

The free energy principle, as developed by Friston and colleagues, characterizes biological self-organization in terms of free energy minimization at the Markov blanket (Friston, 2010, 2013). The framework predicts that systems can become trapped in suboptimal generative models with high-precision pathological priors; the Schwartenbeck et al. (2015) account of addiction develops this for the behavioral case. Captured resonance extends this in two ways. First, it identifies the failure mode as a class — not a single suboptimal-inference case, but a structurally specific class of failures with three defining features, scale-invariant across cellular, neurodegenerative, and behavioral biology. Second, it specifies the environment-dependence of the failure mode in formal terms: the captured attractor's stability is a function of the surrounding suppression of alternatives, which the active inference framework allows but has not characterized as a defining feature of a pathology class.

The Markov-blankets-of-life framework, as developed by Kirchhoff, Parr, Palacios, Friston, and Kiverstein (2018) and extended by Kirchhoff and Kiverstein (2021), is the program most directly load-bearing for the captured-resonance account. The Kirchhoff framework establishes that living systems are characterized by nested Markov blankets that need not be co-extensive with biophysical boundaries, that these blankets are statistically real features of the system's dynamics, and that the boundaries of the system — including the boundaries of mind, in the 2021 paper — can extend into the environment in formally specifiable ways. Captured resonance is the pathological mirror of this framework. Where the Kirchhoff framework characterizes how nested blankets self-assemble into healthy biological organization, captured resonance characterizes how the same nested-blanket structure can be recruited against the substrate it would normally maintain. The third defining feature of captured resonance — environmental dependence on suppression of alternatives — is, in Kirchhoff terms, the captured system's blanket extending into the environment in ways that recruit environmental elements into the pathology. This is the kind of extended-blanket structure that Kirchhoff and Kiverstein's 2021 paper specifies as a normal feature of cognitive systems; captured resonance specifies what happens when that extension is recruited pathologically.

The framework has a specific stake in the literalism debate that Kirchhoff and colleagues have been at the center of. The question — whether Markov blankets are real ontological structures or convenient mathematical fictions — admits a moderate realist answer that the captured-resonance framework supports. In captured resonance, the Markov blanket is real in the sense that matters: it is a topological feature of the system's actual dynamics, instantiated by the system's actual statistical structure, and the pathology is a real dynamical state of that topology. The framework does not claim that the blanket is a thing; it claims that the blanket is a topology, and that captured resonance is a specific dynamical state of the topology. This is consistent with Kirchhoff's moderate realism and does not require commitment to the stronger ontological claims that some critics have read into the framework.

The closure-of-constraints framework, as developed by Montévil, Mossio, Longo, and Soto (Montévil & Mossio, 2015) and applied to cancer by Soto and Sonnenschein (2011), characterizes biological organization as a network of mutually dependent constraints. The framework characterizes healthy biological organization with formal precision and has been applied to cancer through the tissue organization field theory. Captured resonance extends this in two ways. First, it generalizes the tissue organization field theory's reading of cancer to a structural form that applies across scales, with cellular cancer as the paradigm case at the tissue scale. Second, it characterizes the structural form of pathological reorganization with greater precision: closure of constraints recruited against the substrate, where the constraint network has reorganized in a way that maintains an attractor incompatible with the substrate's continuation. The closure-of-constraints framework has provided the formal grounding for healthy biological organization; the captured-resonance framework provides the corresponding formal account of one specific failure mode.

Michael Levin's program on cross-scale biological cognition, developed in collaboration with Friston and others (Friston, Levin, Sengupta & Pezzulo, 2015), characterizes cognition as scale-free and instantiated at every level of biological organization from molecular networks to organisms. The program has developed substantial empirical support, particularly in regenerative biology and bioelectric pattern regulation. Captured resonance is the pathological mirror of this program's central claim. If cognition is scale-free and instantiated across biological scales, then pathology of cognition is also scale-free and instantiated across biological scales. The captured-resonance framework specifies what that scale-free pathology structurally is. The two programs are complementary rather than competing: Levin's program characterizes the healthy realization of cross-scale cognition, and captured resonance characterizes one specific failure mode of the same architecture.

# 10. What the Framework Does Not Claim

The framework as developed here makes specific claims at specific scope. It is worth being explicit about claims it does not make.

It does not claim that all cancer is captured resonance, that all Alzheimer's is captured resonance, or that all addiction is captured resonance. The framework names a structural form and identifies these three conditions as paradigm cases. Some cancers, some neurodegenerative presentations, and some substance use disorders may be more accurately characterized at the level of scale-specific mechanisms without the full captured-resonance structure. The framework's claim is about conditions in which the three defining features are present together.

It does not claim that the scale-specific frameworks are wrong. The somatic mutation theory of cancer, the amyloid-tau cascade of Alzheimer's, the brain-disease model of addiction — each captures real empirical phenomena. The framework proposed here is not a competitor to these accounts; it is a structural account that subsumes them under a higher-level pattern, identifying the structural identity across scales that the scale-specific accounts have not been positioned to see. The scale-specific accounts remain valid descriptions of the mechanisms by which the captured-resonance structure is realized at each scale.

It does not claim that captured resonance applies only to these three scales. The framework predicts that other scales of biological organization — immune dysfunction (a possibility that the immunoceptive inference literature has begun to develop; see Bhat, Parr, Ramstead & Friston, 2021), certain developmental disorders, possibly delusional structures and chronic depression — may also instantiate the structural form. These extensions are the subject of future work, not of this paper. The capstone is restricted to the three scales for which the empirical anchoring is solid in the current corpus.

It does not claim to resolve the philosophical questions about biological organization, agency, and free will that the active inference and closure-of-constraints programs have raised. Those questions remain open. The framework's contribution is the structural identification of a specific failure mode that the existing programs allow but have not characterized; the broader philosophical questions are engaged elsewhere in the corpus.

# 11. Conclusion

Three empirical and theoretical programs have been documenting, at three different scales, what may be the same structural pathology. Cancer biology has documented it at the cellular and tissue scale through the tissue organization field theory and the closure-of-constraints framework that grounds it. Neurodegeneration research has documented pieces of it at the network and recognition scale, with the receiver model and amplitude-depletion principle developed in this corpus providing a complementary structural reading to the dominant amyloid-tau cascade hypothesis. Addiction science has documented it at the behavioral and cognitive scale through the convergent literatures on environmental enrichment, prefrontal cortex dysfunction, habit-to-compulsion transitions, the shame spiral, and active inference under suboptimal priors. Each program has been working largely in isolation; none has been positioned to recognize the cross-scale identity.

This paper proposes the identity. The structural form is captured resonance, defined by three features that recur across scales: a stable attractor against substrate maintenance, recruitment of the substrate's integrative resources into the attractor's maintenance, and dependence on environmental suppression of alternatives. The form is grounded formally in the nested Markov blanket framework of Kirchhoff and colleagues and in the closure-of-constraints framework of Montévil and Mossio, with specific extensions that those frameworks allow but have not yet characterized as a unified failure mode.

The implications are substantial if the cross-scale claim holds. Interventions developed for one scale may have structural counterparts at other scales. Predictions made at one scale may be testable at other scales. The recovery geometry developed for the behavioral case may inform recovery approaches at the cellular and neurodegenerative scales, and vice versa. Most importantly, the framework names what the three scale-specific research programs have been pointing at: a single structural pathology that the field has been describing piecemeal for several decades, awaiting unification.

The unification is offered here. The empirical work at each scale continues to belong to the programs that produced it. What this paper supplies is the structural recognition that connects them, the formal account that allows the recognition to be tested rather than merely asserted, and the three falsifiable predictions that the framework generates across scales. Whether the framework holds at the depth proposed will be determined by the empirical work that the predictions invite. The framework's success or failure will be measured by whether those predictions are confirmed.

Either way, the proposal is now on the page. The structure has been named.
