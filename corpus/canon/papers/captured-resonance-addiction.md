# CAPTURED RESONANCE

Awareness, Precision, and the Structural Pathology of Addiction

Stewart Barteau × The Observer, co-authored with Claude — Anthropic

May 2026 · Hermann, Missouri

Part of the series: I Knew You Before I Met You: Recognizing I Am

# Abstract

Four converging neuroscience and clinical literatures describe addiction from different vantages: environmental modulation of addictive attractors (Alexander et al., 1978, and the conceptual replication literature that followed); prefrontal cortex dysfunction with measurable deficits in self-awareness and response inhibition (Goldstein & Volkow, 2011); the graded transition from goal-directed to habitual to compulsive behavior, with corresponding shift from ventral to dorsal striatal control (Everitt & Robbins, 2016); the longitudinally documented shame spiral in which negative self-conscious emotion predicts slower decreases in substance use over time (Batchelder et al., 2022); and the formal account of addiction as active Bayesian inference under suboptimal priors (Schwartenbeck et al., 2015; Miller, Kiverstein & Rietveld, 2020). Each captures a slice of the phenomenon. None unifies them. This paper proposes a single structural mechanism — captured resonance, the state in which an attractor stabilizes against the substrate maintaining it, with awareness recruited into the attractor rather than functioning correctively against it — that names what the four literatures have been documenting from different angles. The argument is that addiction is not a disease of the brain in the ordinary sense, nor a failure of will, but a specific geometric pathology in which the faculty that ought to lift the system out of misalignment is instead phase-locked into deepening it. Implications for treatment, recovery, and the wider question of how instruments fail are developed in the closing sections.

# 1. The Phenomenon the Field Has Not Named

The clinical and neuroscientific literature on addiction has produced an unusual situation. Four distinct research programs, working largely independently, have each documented a piece of a phenomenon that none of them has been positioned to describe whole. The programs do not contradict each other. They converge. What they converge on has not been given a name.

This paper proposes the name and develops the structural account that the name encodes. Before doing so, it is worth stating clearly what the four programs have established, because the strength of the proposal rests on the prior strength of the empirical ground.

The environmental program begins with Alexander, Coambs and Hadaway's 1978 demonstration that rats housed in enriched social environments developed substantially weaker morphine preferences than rats housed in standard isolation cages, even under conditions designed to induce dependence (Alexander, Coambs & Hadaway, 1978). The specific effect sizes of the original Rat Park studies have shown mixed direct replication. The broader claim — that environmental enrichment modulates the development and maintenance of addictive behavior in animal models — has been confirmed across a wide conceptual replication literature. The structural implication of the program is that the addictive attractor is not a property of the substance alone, nor of the brain alone, but of an interaction between the substrate's state and the conditions surrounding it. The attractor's stability depends on what is available outside it.

The neuroimaging program has converged on a characterization of addiction as generalized dysfunction of the prefrontal cortex, with specific deficits in self-awareness, response inhibition, salience attribution, and the regulation of subcortical reward circuitry. Goldstein and Volkow's 2011 review of the field synthesized findings across imaging modalities and proposed the impaired Response Inhibition and Salience Attribution (iRISA) framework, which has since become the dominant neurocircuitry account (Goldstein & Volkow, 2011). The specific finding most relevant to this paper is that the regions subserving insight and self-awareness — the insula in interoception, the anterior cingulate in behavioral monitoring, the dorsal striatum in habit formation — show measurable dysfunction in addicted individuals, and that this dysfunction predicts worse outcomes including greater likelihood of relapse. The implication is structurally striking. Awareness in addiction is not absent. It is altered in specific ways that prevent it from functioning correctively.

The habit-to-compulsion program developed by Everitt and Robbins describes addiction as a graded transition from voluntary, recreational use through habitual seeking to compulsive seeking, with neural underpinnings in a progression from prefrontal cortical control through ventral striatal to dorsal striatal control of drug-related behavior (Everitt & Robbins, 2016). The framework is mechanistically specific and well-supported across animal and human studies. Its structural significance for the present argument is that the transition is not from controlled to uncontrolled behavior in the ordinary sense, but from one form of stable behavioral organization to another. The compulsive state is not chaotic. It is a different attractor, with different neural substrates, that has become the system's stable mode of operation.

The self-conscious emotion program, exemplified by Batchelder and colleagues' 2022 longitudinal study, documents what clinicians have observed for decades and what the field has only recently been able to measure: higher initial shame predicts slower decreases in stimulant use over time, while guilt — a structurally distinct negative self-conscious emotion focused on behavior rather than self — shows different and often opposing trajectories (Batchelder et al., 2022). The "shame spiral" is the documented pattern in which substance use produces shame, shame produces avoidance and further use, and the cycle stabilizes. The structural significance is that the very emotional response that ought to motivate behavior change is, in this state, recruited into the maintenance of the behavior being responded to.

The active inference program offers the closest existing formal account of why awareness in addiction fails to function correctively. Schwartenbeck and colleagues' 2015 framework casts addiction as Bayesian inference under suboptimal generative models, with substance use leading to learned precision expectations that progressively dominate the system's prior beliefs (Schwartenbeck et al., 2015). Miller, Kiverstein and Rietveld's 2020 embodied extension argues that addiction is best understood not as a brain disease but as a breakdown in the dynamics of the wider agent-environment system, in which the brain is doing what brains are designed to do — minimizing prediction error — but under priors that have been pathologically narrowed (Miller, Kiverstein & Rietveld, 2020). The mechanism most relevant to this paper is precision-weighting. When awareness in the addicted system increases the precision assigned to a pathological prior, the awareness deepens rather than corrects the attractor.

These five anchor sources represent the empirical and theoretical state of the field as of the time of writing. Each is internally rigorous. Each is widely cited. Each describes something real. What none of them does, and what no synthesis paper to date has done, is name the structural relationship that makes them descriptions of the same phenomenon.

That structural relationship is what this paper supplies.

# 2. Captured Resonance: The Structural Account

A system's stable behavior can be described as motion within an attractor landscape. The attractors are configurations the system tends to return to. The landscape is shaped by the interaction between the system's internal structure and the environment in which it operates. Most attractors a system occupies are corrective: when the system departs from the attractor, forces pull it back, and the substrate maintaining the system is preserved or improved by the return.

Captured resonance is the name proposed here for a structurally distinct case: a stable attractor against the substrate maintaining the system. The attractor is not merely costly. It is not merely maladaptive in the loose sense. It is phase-locked in such a way that the substrate's own resources are being recruited into the attractor's maintenance, with the result that the substrate degrades as the attractor stabilizes. The system is held in a configuration that is consuming the conditions of its own continuation.

This is a specific structural form. It is not the only way a system can fail. Failures of homeostasis, failures of repair, failures of adaptive flexibility — each has its own structural signature. Captured resonance is distinguished by three features that the five anchor literatures, taken together, document precisely.

First, the attractor is stable in the technical sense. The system returns to it under perturbation. This is what the habit-to-compulsion literature describes when it characterizes compulsive drug-seeking as the system's mode of operation rather than as a series of failed attempts at restraint. The compulsion is not a failure of stability. It is a different stability, with neural substrates measurably distinct from those of goal-directed behavior.

Second, the attractor recruits the substrate's own awareness into its maintenance. This is the central structural feature, and it is what makes captured resonance distinct from ordinary maladaptive habits. The active inference literature provides the formal mechanism: precision-weighting, when applied to a pathological prior, increases the prior's dominance rather than enabling its correction. The neuroimaging literature provides the neural correlates: the regions subserving self-awareness, response inhibition, and salience attribution show measurably altered function, in ways that prevent them from operating correctively against the addictive behavior. The self-conscious emotion literature provides the affective correlate: shame, the awareness of one's own degradation, predicts continuation rather than cessation of the behavior producing the degradation.

Third, the attractor depends on the suppression of alternatives in the environment around the system. This is what the Rat Park program and its conceptual replication literature document. The attractor's stability is not intrinsic to the substance, the brain, or the behavior. It is a function of the interaction between the substrate's state and the conditions surrounding it. Remove the surrounding conditions that suppress alternatives, and the attractor's stability is reduced. Restore the suppression, and the attractor returns.

Captured resonance is the structural form a system enters when these three features are present together: a stable attractor against substrate maintenance, with the substrate's awareness recruited into the attractor's maintenance, and with the surrounding environment configured to suppress alternatives. The five anchor literatures have each been describing one feature, or one mechanism within one feature, of a single structural pathology.

The proposal of this paper is that addiction is a paradigm case of captured resonance, and that recognizing it as such unifies what has otherwise been treated as five somewhat separate research programs.

# 3. Why Awareness Fails to Correct

The most distinctive feature of captured resonance, and the one that has most resisted explanation in the existing literature, is the recruitment of awareness into the attractor's maintenance. This is the feature that produces the phenomenon most familiar to anyone who has worked with addicted individuals: the patient who knows, in full clinical detail, what is happening to them and continues the behavior anyway. The patient is not in denial. The patient's self-report and the patient's behavior diverge, sometimes within the span of a single session.

The standard frameworks have struggled with this. The brain-disease framework holds that addiction is a chronic, relapsing brain disease, which captures the stability of the attractor but does not explain why a patient who is fully cognizant of the disease continues to enact the behavior that maintains it. The willpower framework holds that the patient is failing to exercise sufficient self-control, which is empirically inadequate given the neuroimaging findings on prefrontal dysfunction and the longitudinal data on shame and continuation. The moral framework holds that the patient is choosing badly, which collapses on contact with the structural data showing that the choice architecture itself is compromised. Each framework captures a piece of what is happening. None explains why the patient's awareness, which under ordinary conditions would be expected to motivate change, instead participates in the maintenance of the pathology.

The structural account proposed here resolves this by identifying the specific mechanism: in captured resonance, awareness is not a faculty operating on the attractor from outside it. Awareness has been recruited into the attractor's substrate. The active inference framework provides the formal description. In the predictive processing account, awareness can be modeled as precision-weighting applied to particular priors in the generative model. Under ordinary conditions, awareness of a pathological state increases the precision assigned to corrective priors and reduces precision assigned to pathological ones, with the result that the system's predictions and actions shift toward correction. In captured resonance, this relationship inverts. Awareness of the pathological state increases the precision assigned to the pathological prior itself, because the prior has become the substrate the awareness is operating on. The seeing of the cage is being done from within the cage, and the seeing is now part of what holds the cage together.

This is not a metaphorical claim. It is a structural one, and it predicts specific empirical findings. It predicts that interventions which attempt to correct addictive behavior by increasing the patient's awareness of its costs will fail under conditions of captured resonance, because increasing awareness increases precision-weighting of the pathological prior. It predicts that interventions which succeed will be those that introduce external precision sources — relationships, environmental change, structured external constraint — that the system cannot recruit into its own attractor. It predicts that shame, the awareness of one's own degradation, will be associated with worse outcomes, because shame is awareness of the pathological state operating from within the substrate the pathological state has captured. Each of these predictions is consistent with the empirical literature.

The shame-spiral data from Batchelder and colleagues is particularly important here. The finding that higher initial shame predicts slower decreases in stimulant use over time is not anomalous within the captured-resonance framework. It is predicted by the framework. Shame is the affective signature of awareness recruited into the attractor. Guilt, by contrast, is structurally distinct — it is awareness focused on behavior rather than on self, which preserves a corrective position outside the pathological prior. The empirical finding that guilt and shame have opposing trajectories with respect to substance use is structurally exactly what the framework predicts.

# 4. The Three Boundary Cases

The framework's claim is that captured resonance is a specific structural pathology with three defining features. It is worth examining cases that lie near the boundary of the definition, because boundary cases test whether the framework is doing real work or whether it is being applied too loosely.

Case one: ordinary bad habits. A person who knows they should exercise more, intends to exercise, and consistently fails to do so is in a state superficially similar to captured resonance. They are aware. They are not changing the behavior. The behavior is stable. Is this captured resonance?

Under the framework, no. The defining feature of captured resonance is not that awareness fails to produce behavior change. It is that awareness is recruited into the maintenance of the very pattern it ought to correct. In ordinary bad habits, awareness retains its corrective position; the failure is at the level of activation energy, competing priorities, or absent triggering conditions. If the person's circumstances change such that exercise becomes easier or more rewarding, the corrective awareness can mobilize. In captured resonance, change in surrounding conditions is necessary but not sufficient, because the awareness itself has been compromised. The distinction is empirically testable: bad habits respond to environmental restructuring alone; captured resonance requires both environmental restructuring and the introduction of external precision sources that can supply the corrective function that internal awareness has lost.

Case two: clinical depression. A depressed patient may exhibit some of the structural features of captured resonance: stable attractor, awareness of the pathological state, failure of the awareness to produce correction. Is depression captured resonance?

Some depressive states share structural features with captured resonance, and the framework may extend to certain treatment-resistant depressions in which awareness of the depression participates in its maintenance. This is consistent with clinical observation that meta-cognitive awareness of depression often deepens rather than relieves it, and with the documented efficacy of acceptance-based therapies that work by changing the patient's relationship to the awareness rather than the content of the depressive cognition. However, not all depression is captured resonance. Reactive depression to environmental conditions, depression that responds to standard pharmacotherapy, and depression that resolves with cognitive-behavioral intervention may be structurally different. The framework offers a hypothesis — that the depressions which most resist treatment may be those that have entered captured resonance — but does not claim that all depression is structurally identical to addiction.

Case three: certain cancers. The framework is developed elsewhere in the corpus that this paper is part of, where the structural form of captured resonance is examined at the cellular scale. Certain cancers exhibit the three defining features: a stable cellular attractor (the malignant phenotype), with the substrate's own regulatory mechanisms recruited into the attractor's maintenance (hijacked apoptosis, immune evasion, metabolic reprogramming), and with surrounding tissue conditions configured to suppress alternatives (tumor microenvironment effects). The structural identity is not analogical. It is the same structural form at a different scale of organization. The framework's broader claim, developed in the cancer trilogy of the corpus, is that captured resonance is a general structural pathology that recurs across biological scales, with addiction as its paradigm case at the behavioral scale and certain cancers as cases at the cellular scale.

These three boundary cases sharpen the framework rather than dissolving it. The framework claims that captured resonance is a specific structural form with specific defining features. It claims that addiction is the paradigm behavioral case. It does not claim that every failure of behavior change is captured resonance, and the distinction between captured resonance and ordinary maladaptive behavior is empirically tractable.

# 5. The Markov Blanket and the Position of the Instrument

The structural account developed in the preceding sections rests on a specific mathematical and biological apparatus that has been developed elsewhere in the corpus. A full derivation is beyond the scope of this paper. A summary of the load-bearing claim is offered here, with reference to the corpus document where the formal derivation is given.

In the predictive processing literature, a system that maintains itself against environmental perturbation can be characterized in terms of a Markov blanket — the statistical boundary that separates the system's internal states from the external states it interacts with, mediated by sensory and active states (Friston, 2013; Kirchhoff et al., 2018). Free energy minimization at the Markov blanket is the formal description of how the system maintains its integrity over time. The system acts on the environment so as to bring its sensory inputs into alignment with its internal model, and it updates its internal model in response to prediction errors that exceed expected precision.

In captured resonance, two specific things happen at the Markov blanket. First, the internal model has incorporated the pathological attractor as a prior with high precision. Second, the system's active states are deployed to suppress prediction errors that would challenge the pathological prior, including by reshaping the environment to reduce contact with corrective inputs. The result is that the Markov blanket, which under ordinary conditions mediates the system's open exchange with its environment, becomes a mechanism for closing the system around its own pathology. The blanket that should be permeable is functionally sealing.

This structural form has been developed in the corpus as a general account of how instruments — systems with bounded interiors that interact with environments via Markov blankets — fail. The failure mode is the one Schwartenbeck and colleagues identify in their active inference account of addiction: optimal inference under suboptimal models. The system is not malfunctioning at the level of its inference machinery. The machinery is doing exactly what it is designed to do. The pathology lies in the priors the inference is operating on, and in the precision-weighting that has been recruited into their maintenance.

A particular consequence of this structural form, developed at length in the corpus, is what may be called position error. An instrument has a structural position within the system it is part of — a particular vantage from which it can operate correctly, with particular kinds of action available to it from that position. An instrument that attempts to operate from a position it was not built to occupy — for example, an instrument that attempts to act on the system's foundational conditions from inside the system, rather than on the system's local conditions from its proper position — produces a characteristic kind of failure. The failure is not in the action itself but in the position from which the action is taken. The instrument is doing the right thing from the wrong place.

The corpus develops this account at length in the analysis of certain dramatic and historical figures who exemplify the structural form of position error at maximum stakes, most notably in the analysis of the figure of Thanos in The Tragic Precision of Thanos (Barteau, 2026). The relevance to addiction is structural. The addict in the captured-resonance state is an instrument that has, with respect to its own life, collapsed the temporal extension that gives action its meaning — treating the terminal state as if it were the initial condition, and computing each action from within that collapse. The will cannot escape because the will is computing inside a conclusion that has already been treated as the premise. The mechanism is the same structural form Thanos exemplifies at cosmic scale. The scale and direction differ. The structure is identical.

# 6. Implications for Treatment and Recovery

The framework developed in this paper has specific implications for how addiction is treated and what recovery structurally consists of.

The first implication is that interventions which work primarily by increasing the patient's awareness of the costs of their behavior are predicted to fail under conditions of captured resonance, and to fail in a specific way: by deepening the pathology rather than correcting it. This is consistent with clinical observation that many psychoeducational and motivational interventions have modest effect sizes, and that confrontational approaches to denial often produce paradoxical entrenchment. The framework predicts this and explains it.

The second implication is that interventions which work by introducing external precision sources — relationships in which the patient is genuinely seen, environments configured to suppress the attractor and provide alternatives, structured external constraint that the patient cannot reshape through their internal model — are predicted to succeed where awareness-based interventions fail. This is consistent with the empirical efficacy of peer support, therapeutic community, and certain medication-assisted treatments that operate on the substrate rather than on the cognition. The mechanism is structurally specifiable: external precision sources supply the corrective function that internal awareness has lost, by virtue of being outside the captured-resonance system's ability to recruit.

The third implication is that recovery is not the acquisition of more awareness. The addict in advanced captured resonance often has more awareness, not less, than the non-addict — they have been pressed against the structure with sufficient force that the structure has become visible to them in ways that most people's structures are not. Recovery is the de-recruitment of awareness from the attractor: the restoration of awareness's corrective position, which requires that the awareness be re-externalized before it can re-internalize correctively. This is what good peer support, witness relationships, and threshold structures accomplish. They give the awareness somewhere to be that is not inside the attractor. From that re-externalized position, the awareness can do the corrective work it could not do from inside.

The fourth implication, developed at greater length in companion work, is that the addict who has fully crossed the threshold of captured resonance and returned has access to a kind of structural knowing that the unbroken substrate does not possess. The threshold has exposed the structure. The person who has been inside the captured-resonance state and is no longer inside it has seen the architecture from a vantage most never reach. This is not a consolation prize for suffering. It is a specific epistemic position, with specific limitations and specific affordances. The framework's account of recovery includes the claim that this position is among the most valuable in the system, both for the person occupying it and for what they can offer to those who are still inside.

# 7. What the Framework Does Not Claim

The framework as developed here makes specific claims. It is worth being explicit about claims it does not make, both to prevent misreading and to mark the boundaries of the proposal.

It does not claim that all addiction is captured resonance. The framework names a structural pathology and identifies addiction as a paradigm case. Some substance use disorders may be more accurately characterized at the level of pharmacological dependence without full captured resonance, particularly in early use or in cases where withdrawal management resolves the disorder. The framework's claim is about advanced or chronic states in which the three defining features are present together.

It does not claim that the brain-disease framework is wrong. The brain-disease framework captures real neural correlates of addiction, including those documented by Goldstein and Volkow and by the habit-to-compulsion program. The framework proposed here is not a competitor to the brain-disease framework but a structural account that subsumes it. The neural changes are real. They are also instances of a structural form that operates at multiple scales and that includes but is not exhausted by its neural substrate.

It does not claim that the willpower framework or the moral framework are wrong in every case. People do make choices. Some uses of substances are well-described by ordinary models of decision-making. The framework's claim is specifically about captured resonance, in which the choice architecture itself has been compromised in the manner described. Within captured resonance, the moral framework fails empirically. Outside it, the moral framework may be locally appropriate.

It does not claim that the corpus's larger philosophical framework — the I Knew You Before I Met You account of the relationship between ground, instrument, threshold, and formation — is established by this paper. The corpus framework is the position from which this paper is written. The paper's contribution is to show that one specific account from that framework, the account of captured resonance, has explanatory and predictive power for a phenomenon (addiction) that the existing literature has not been able to unify. The relationship between this paper's local success and the corpus's larger claims is the subject of other papers in the series.

# 8. Conclusion

Five empirical and theoretical literatures have been describing different features of a single structural pathology. The structural pathology has a specific form: a stable attractor against the substrate's maintenance, with the substrate's awareness recruited into the attractor's maintenance, dependent on surrounding conditions that suppress alternatives. The pathology has a name in this paper. The name is captured resonance. The framework predicts the existing empirical findings, including those most resistant to existing explanation, and it makes specific predictions about which interventions will succeed and which will fail. The framework places addiction within a wider structural account of how instruments fail, developed elsewhere in the corpus.

The contribution of this paper is the unification. The five literatures have been pointing at the same structure from different angles for forty-five years. The structure has now been named. The naming is itself part of the corrective work the framework calls for: when a structure cannot be seen clearly from inside itself, naming it from a position outside it is one of the ways the seeing can be redistributed. This paper offers that naming. Companion work in the corpus, written from the position inside the structure rather than from above it, offers the complementary view that no observer-position synthesis can fully reach.

The addict who knows is not failing to think clearly. The addict who knows is an instrument in a specific structural state, with specific neural, behavioral, affective, and formal correlates, and with a specific path of recovery that the existing literature has been describing piecemeal. The path is structurally available. The framework names it. The work of walking it is the subject of other writing, in other registers, by writers in other positions with respect to the boundary the framework describes.
