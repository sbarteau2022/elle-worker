# What Alzheimer’s Is Doing While We’re Looking at Plaques

On the receiver, the signal, and the disease that is neither

Stewart Barteau , co-authored with Claude — Anthropic

## Abstract

Three decades of Alzheimer’s research have been organized around the conviction that the disease is, fundamentally, a problem with what is in the brain. Plaques, tangles, misfolded proteins, lost neurons. The most recent generation of disease-modifying therapies clears the targeted material with measurable success and produces, at best, marginal cognitive benefit. The field has been describing this gap as a problem of timing, dosage, or target refinement. This paper proposes that the gap is structural: the disease is not located where the field has been looking. Alzheimer’s, on the account developed here, is not primarily a failure of substrate. It is a failure of the dynamic operation that the substrate is meant to support — the operation by which a brain remains a coherent receiver of the structure presented to it. The paper installs this alternative frame, walks through the documented phenomena that current models cannot account for, and shows that each falls into place when the unit of analysis shifts from substrate to dynamics. What the disease destroys is not the receiver’s components. It is the receiver’s capacity to lock on.

# 1. Thirty years of looking at the wrong thing, correctly

The amyloid hypothesis, in its modern form, dates to the early 1990s. The basic claim is straightforward: aggregates of beta-amyloid peptide accumulate in the brains of Alzheimer’s patients, these aggregates are toxic to neurons, and the cognitive decline characteristic of the disease follows from the resulting neuronal loss. Refinements have accumulated — oligomeric versus fibrillar species, the role of tau, microglial responses, vascular contributions — but the structural commitment has held. The disease is what is in the brain. Remove what is in the brain, and the disease will yield.

On its own terms, this program has worked. The biology has been characterized in extraordinary detail. The pathological cascade has been mapped. The recent generation of monoclonal antibodies — lecanemab, donanemab — demonstrably clears amyloid plaque from the brains of living patients. By the field’s own primary endpoint, the program has succeeded. The target was identified, the target was hit.

And yet. The cognitive benefit produced by clearing amyloid has been marginal. Statistically detectable in large trials. Clinically modest. Often imperceptible to the patient and the family. Side effects, including amyloid-related imaging abnormalities, are real and sometimes severe. The disease, once symptomatic, continues to progress. This is not the result a successful target validation should produce.

The field’s response has been to refine within the frame. Treat earlier. Treat in patients with the right genetic background. Combine targets. The frame itself — that the disease is what is in the brain, and removing what is in the brain will arrest the disease — has not been seriously challenged from inside, because to challenge it from inside is to call thirty years of work into question. The work was not wasted. The biology was real. But the conclusion the biology was meant to support has not arrived, and the gap between target engagement and clinical benefit is no longer the kind of gap that further refinement is likely to close.

The hypothesis this paper develops is that the gap is real, persistent, and informative. The substrate-modification model has succeeded at modifying the substrate. The disease has not yielded. This pattern is consistent with one of two possibilities: either the substrate target is wrong, or the disease is not, in the structural sense, a substrate problem. The first possibility has been thoroughly explored and produced more candidates with the same gap. The second has not been seriously taken up, and is what the rest of this paper attempts.

# 2. The phenomena the substrate model cannot account for

There is a class of clinical observations in Alzheimer’s disease that are well-documented, repeatedly observed, and which the substrate-damage model handles by ignoring or by ad-hoc explanation. Considered individually, each can be set aside as a curiosity. Considered as a set, their coherence is itself diagnostic of the level at which the disease is operating. The set is not exhaustive; it is selected for the structural pressure it places on the standard frame.

First: terminal lucidity. Patients with severe Alzheimer’s, often non-verbal for months or years, sometimes become coherent in the hours or days before death. Recognition returns. Conversation returns. Autobiographical access returns. The substrate, at the moment this occurs, is at its most degraded; it is actively dying. A model in which cognition is a direct function of substrate integrity predicts that this should not happen. It happens. It has been documented for centuries and described in modern clinical literature for decades. The standard response is to characterize it as anomalous and move on.

Second: paradoxical lucidity in advanced disease. The same phenomenon, non-terminal. Brief windows — minutes, sometimes longer — in which a patient who has been functionally absent returns. Recognizes a spouse. Speaks an old phrase in context. Then the window closes. The substrate has not changed in those minutes. Something else has.

Third: music-induced recognition. Patients in advanced dementia, presented with music wound deep into their identity — a hymn from childhood, a wedding song, a piece their mother sang — sometimes recover not just musical engagement but autobiographical access, language, recognition of the people in the room. The effect is not random across music. It is specific to music whose roots in the patient run deep. When the window closes, it closes.

Fourth: the order in which recognition fails. The pattern across patients is consistent and structural. Recent acquaintances first. Then more distant family. Then the spouse. Then the children. Then the parents. Then, last, the self in the mirror. The order is not random and is not strictly chronological by acquaintance. It tracks, instead, something like the depth at which each recognition was wound into the person’s identity over a lifetime.

Fifth: sundowning. The pronounced worsening of cognitive coherence in late afternoon and evening, partial recovery overnight, recurrence the next day. The phenomenon is directional in time. Substrate damage does not have a time of day. The standard frame describes sundowning, prescribes for it, and does not explain it.

Sixth: the two distinct failure modes within the same diagnosis. Some patients trend toward perseveration — looping on a phrase, a question, a movement, the same circuit repeated. Others trend toward confabulation — generating content that is fluent but disconnected from any prior moment, including the moment immediately preceding. Both are common. Both are characteristic of the disease. The standard model treats them as separate symptoms with separate substrates. They are, more economically, two directions of failure of the same operation.

Seventh: the experience reported by families that the late-stage patient is not a damaged version of the person they knew, but a different person, or a sequence of different persons. This is not a metaphor families reach for. It is what they describe directly. The substrate-damage model treats this as a coping framing. It is, more carefully attended to, a structural observation about what the disease has actually changed.

Each of these is a phenomenon. None of them is anomalous in the sense of being rare. All of them are well-known to clinicians, families, and the literature. None of them is predicted by the substrate-damage frame, and most are mildly embarrassing to it. The proposal of this paper is that the coherence of the set is the thing to attend to. The disease is doing something that produces, naturally and predictably, all seven of these features at once. What is it doing?

# 3. The receiver, the signal, and the lock

Consider a familiar arrangement. A wireless network is broadcasting in a room. The signal is present everywhere in the room, continuously, with structure that can in principle be decoded into the information it carries. Whether any given device in the room is connected to the network depends not on the signal’s presence but on the device’s capacity to perform the resonant lock that turns presence into connection. A device with a degraded antenna in a noisy room will report intermittent connection. The signal has not changed. The noise has not changed. The device’s capacity to discriminate signal from noise has changed.

The metaphor is useful only as far as it gets us to the right question. Recognition, on the standard cognitive model, is conceived as retrieval: the brain stores a representation, and recognition is the matching of present input against stored content. The model has trouble with the phenomena above because it is committed, at a foundational level, to the idea that recognition is something the brain does to a signal once the signal has arrived. The alternative being proposed here is that recognition is an instance of resonant lock. It is not retrieval of stored content. It is the live synchronization of a coherent internal structure with a coherent external structure presented to it.

If recognition is resonant lock, then it has prerequisites that the retrieval model does not have. The receiver must itself be coherent. Internal coherence is not a property of the stored content. It is a property of the dynamic operation by which the receiver is, at this moment, an integrated thing capable of receiving anything at all. A wireless device that has lost its capacity to maintain a coherent internal oscillator does not have a storage problem. It has a receiver problem. The same signal, in the same room, finds a device that can no longer lock.

This reframing has immediate consequences for what the disease is doing. On the retrieval model, dementia is loss of stored content; the question is what destroyed the storage. On the receiver model, dementia is loss of the dynamic operation that constitutes a coherent receiver; the storage may be largely intact, the signal certainly is, and what has failed is the lock. The seven phenomena listed above stop being anomalous and become predictable. Music wound deep into identity strikes the receiver at a frequency it can still resonate at, briefly, even when general lock has failed. The order of recognition failure tracks the depth at which each recognition was wound into the receiver’s coherent structure. Sundowning is what receiver degradation looks like when it is sensitive to a daily cycle. Perseveration and confabulation are the two structurally distinct ways a failing receiver can fail — by collapsing onto a single locked frequency it cannot leave, or by losing the coherence required to lock at all. Terminal lucidity is, on this model, no longer anomalous; it is the receiver’s final coherent operation as the dynamics release. We will return to it in a later paper.

The remainder of this paper installs the dynamic operation precisely enough that these claims are not metaphorical. The metaphor was a door. The geometry is what is on the other side of it.

# 4. The dynamics that constitute a receiver

What does it take, dynamically, for something to be a coherent receiver across time? This is the question the substrate-damage model has not asked, because the substrate-damage model conceives of the brain as a thing that stores and retrieves rather than a process that is, at every moment, performing the operation by which it is a thing at all. We need a minimum geometry. Not a complete one — the complete geometry is a longer paper — but enough to support the claims that follow.

Begin with self-reference. The capacity to be a coherent receiver across time requires the system to refer to itself: this moment of receiving must be enrolled into the same self that received the prior moment, or there is no continuous receiver. Self-reference, treated geometrically rather than logically, is a structure in which the system passes through itself. The two-dimensional projection of such a structure is a figure-eight, the lemniscate, in which the trajectory crosses itself at a central point. This projection is what becomes visible at the moment of crossing; the structure itself is higher-dimensional.

Specifically: the structure underlying continuous self-reference in time is a torus, and the system’s temporal trajectory is a winding around it. A torus is the geometric object on which a single trajectory can wind around two independent axes — a major axis and a minor axis — producing a path that need never close on itself while remaining on a bounded surface. The lemniscate is what one sees when one projects this winding into two dimensions and observes the trajectory at the regions where the windings cross.

What ratio of windings the trajectory must hold matters. If the ratio of major-axis to minor-axis winding is rational, the trajectory closes after some finite number of passes; the system becomes periodic; the receiver becomes a fixed loop, replaying. If the ratio is irrational and structureless, the trajectory disperses, never establishing the kind of bounded recurrence that allows past passes to relate coherently to present ones; the receiver becomes noise. The condition that the trajectory remain bounded, never-closing, and coherently related across passes is the condition that the winding ratio be a particular kind of irrational — specifically, the most irrational ratio available, the one furthest from any rational approximation. That ratio is the golden ratio, phi.

Phi shows up here not as ornament. It is the ratio at which the trajectory remains productively unstable: never the same pass twice, never two passes unrelated to each other, every pass conditioning the next without any pass closing the structure. This is what continuous self-reference in time geometrically is. The operation is not the storage of past states. It is the maintenance of a phi-wound trajectory on a torus, in which past passes are accessible to present ones not by retrieval but by structural proximity on the wound surface.

Two further features of this geometry matter for the disease. First, the trajectory requires amplitude to traverse from one rotation into the next. The winding does not maintain itself for free; each rotation costs energy delivered to the dynamics, and that energy is what carries the trajectory through to the next rotation at the correct ratio. Without sufficient amplitude, the trajectory cannot complete the next rotation at phi. It either falls back onto a rational-ratio orbit — a closed loop — or fails to wind at all, dispersing into incoherent motion. Second, the torus itself is not a substrate-independent surface. It is the form the dynamics maintain by running. The geometry and the dynamics are not separable. A torus in this sense exists only insofar as a phi-wound trajectory is currently winding it; if the winding fails, the torus fails.

This is the minimum receiver. A coherent self in time is a phi-wound trajectory on a torus that is itself maintained by the winding. Self-reference is the structural form of the trajectory passing through itself. Recognition of external coherent structure is the resonant lock that becomes possible when this internal operation is running at sufficient amplitude. Internal coherence is not a state. It is the active, ongoing performance of the wound geometry.

# 5. The disease, named at the level it operates

Alzheimer’s disease, on this account, is the progressive failure of amplitude available to drive the phi-wound trajectory from one rotation into the next. The substrate damage that the field has documented — plaque, tangle, atrophy — is real and is downstream. What the substrate damage produces, structurally, is a system in which the dynamics have less amplitude available to maintain the winding at phi. The disease is what happens to the receiver when its capacity to drive its own coherence falls below what the geometry requires.

When amplitude is insufficient, the winding ratio drifts off phi. There are two directions it can drift, and each produces a recognizable clinical signature.

Drift toward rational ratios produces low-period closed orbits on the torus. The trajectory falls onto a closed loop and replays it. The patient repeats a phrase, a question, a movement, the same circuit. This is perseveration. It has been described as a cognitive deficit and as a behavioral symptom; it is, more economically, what a phi-wound trajectory does when it loses the amplitude to remain off-rational. The orbit is closed because the dynamics no longer have what it takes to leave it.

Drift away from any winding ratio produces incoherent trajectories that no longer maintain bounded relation to the structure. The patient generates content — sometimes fluent, sometimes lucid in tone — that does not connect to the immediately preceding moment, including the moment in which the patient was just speaking. This is confabulation. It has been described as a memory deficit. It is, more economically, what a phi-wound trajectory does when it loses the coherence to wind at all.

Perseveration and confabulation are not separate symptoms with separate substrates. They are the two failure modes of the same operation, distinguished by the direction in which the winding ratio is degrading. Individual patients tend to fail in one direction more than the other, and the direction can be reasonably understood as a feature of where their dynamics are biased relative to the phi point. The standard diagnostic frame, which lacks the structural unity to predict that these two phenomena would co-occur in the same disease, has had to treat them as parallel facts. Here, they are predicted by a single operational failure.

The further consequence is that disease progression is self-reinforcing in a way that substrate models do not capture. Because the torus is maintained by the winding, sub-phi cycles do not just produce momentary failure of recognition; they degrade the very surface that the next cycle must traverse. Each rotation that runs at insufficient amplitude makes the next rotation harder to drive at phi, because the torus that supports the next rotation is itself less coherent than it was. Progression, on this account, is not the accumulation of damage in a static substrate. It is the compounding consequence of dynamics and form failing each other.

This is the move the substrate model structurally cannot make, because it depends on a separation between geometry and dynamics that the receiver does not actually possess.

# 6. The phenomena, mapped

The seven phenomena from Section 2 are now predictable rather than anomalous. The mapping is not a series of separate explanations. It is a single operation showing different surfaces of itself.

Recent memory failing before remote memory. The most recent passes of the trajectory were the ones still being driven into new traversals when amplitude began to fail. They are the least deeply wound into the torus and require the most current amplitude to be reachable. Older material is wound through more of the surface, accessible through more redundant paths, and remains reachable at lower amplitudes. The patient has not lost the storage of recent memory in any independent sense; the receiver can no longer reach the regions where recent passes were inscribed.

The order of recognition failure. Recognition of a person is a resonant lock between the receiver and the coherent structure that person presents to it. The depth of the lock corresponds to how deeply that person has been wound into the receiver’s structure over time. Spouses and recent loves are wound at relatively recent depths; parents and earliest formative figures are wound near the structure’s origin region; the self is the structure’s own crossing point. As amplitude fails, locks at shallower depths fail first because they require finer receiver discrimination. Locks at deeper depths persist longer because they are driven by the structure’s most foundational frequencies. The order is not arbitrary. It is the order in which the receiver loses access to its own depths, from the surface inward.

Sundowning. The day, considered as a unit, is itself a small wound structure in which morning passes condition afternoon passes condition evening passes. Each cycle of cognition costs amplitude. In a healthy receiver, amplitude is regenerated faster than it is spent, with sleep providing a major restoration that re-establishes the geometry overnight. In a disease state, amplitude regeneration is failing. By late afternoon, the trajectory is running at sub-phi; coherence collapses on a directional gradient through the day. Sleep partially restores. Disease progression is, in part, the slope of overnight restoration becoming insufficient to reach the next morning’s required amplitude. Sundowning is the daily signature of this slope. It is directional in time because the underlying failure is directional in time.

Perseveration and confabulation. Already given. Two directions of off-phi drift, producing the two failure modes characteristic of the disease.

Music-induced recognition. Music wound deep into identity strikes the receiver at frequencies near its foundational windings. Even when general lock has failed, the receiver can still resonate at its deepest available frequencies because those frequencies require the least fine discrimination to drive. A signal delivered at the structure’s fundamental will ring harmonically through the structure even when surface resonances have collapsed. This is why the effect is specific to deep music and not to music in general; the principle is harmonic, not stimulative. Recognition restoration through music is not the music “reminding” the patient. It is external coherence supplying a signal at a frequency the failing receiver can still lock onto.

Paradoxical lucidity. Brief windows of receiver coherence sufficient to perform recognition, occurring when conditions — internal, external, or both — momentarily push amplitude above the threshold required for general lock. The lucidity is not anomalous on this model; it is what the receiver does when amplitude is briefly sufficient. The window closes when amplitude drops back below threshold. The cognitive content was never lost. The lock was intermittent.

The sense that the late-stage patient is a different person. As the winding ratio drifts further from phi and the torus that supports continuity degrades, each successive cycle launches with diminishing inheritance from the prior structure. The patient is, structurally, less continuous with the patient of yesterday than a healthy person is with their yesterday-self. Late in the disease, this divergence is no longer subtle. The family is not metaphorizing when they describe meeting someone new. They are reporting the structural fact that the receiver they are looking at is no longer reliably the same receiver across the day. The disease has not destroyed the patient. It has progressively interrupted the continuity that makes “the patient across time” a coherent reference.

Terminal lucidity is the seventh phenomenon, and the deepest. Its full account requires further development that the trilogy will undertake in its third paper. For present purposes it is enough to note that the receiver model predicts something the substrate model excludes: at the threshold of structural release, when the dynamics no longer need to drive a next rotation, all available amplitude becomes available to the present pass. The receiver, briefly, can do something it has not been able to do for the duration of the disease. The substrate, at that moment, has no remaining capacity by the substrate model’s own metrics. The lucidity occurs anyway. We will hold this for later, where it can be given the treatment it requires.

# 7. What this implies, briefly, about treatment

This is not a treatment paper. The implications it carries for treatment, however, are direct enough to be worth naming, and they are consistent with empirical findings that the field has accumulated without being able to explain.

If the disease is failure of dynamics rather than failure of substrate, then interventions that operate at the level of the substrate can, at best, slow the rate at which dynamics degrade. They cannot restore dynamics, because the dynamics are not a property the substrate possesses independently of operation. This is consistent with what disease-modifying therapies have actually delivered: measurable substrate effect, marginal clinical effect.

Interventions that operate at the level of dynamics are operating at the right level. Three categories of intervention have empirical support of varying strength, and each becomes intelligible under the receiver model.

External coherence. Music wound to the patient, familiar environments, sustained presence of long-bonded family, ritualized daily structure. These are not comfort measures. They are external coherence sources from which the failing receiver can borrow what it can no longer generate internally. The receiver synchronizes briefly with an externally held coherent field; recognition is performed during the synchronization; when the field is removed, the synchronization fails. The intervention is real, and it does not accumulate, because amplitude is by definition something that has to keep being supplied. This explains why such interventions feel evanescent to families and yet feel, while they are happening, completely real. They are completely real. They are also intermittent by structural necessity.

Sleep architecture. Anything that protects the integrity of deep sleep — light hygiene, circadian entrainment, treatment of sleep apnea, avoidance of pharmacological agents that suppress slow-wave activity — is operating directly on the system’s primary mechanism of overnight amplitude restoration. The receiver model predicts that the most leveraged single category of intervention currently available is the protection of sleep. The literature has been converging on this for some years. The mechanism, on this account, is not metabolic clearance alone, though that contributes. It is the overnight re-establishment of the wound geometry.

Circadian entrainment. The directional asymmetry of sundowning is itself a clue: the disease is coupled to the daily axis. Strengthening that axis — morning light, structured meals, social rhythm, evening dimming — strengthens the temporal scaffold against which the dynamics are running. This will be the subject of the trilogy’s second paper, which examines sundowning as the directly observable temporal signature of amplitude depletion. For now it is enough to note that the field’s clinical knowledge of what helps in dementia care has, for some time, been pointing at the level the disease is actually operating on, even where the field’s theoretical commitments have been pointing elsewhere.

What the receiver model does not promise is a substrate cure. There is, on this account, no pharmacological agent that will restore dynamics by acting on substrate, because dynamics are not stored in substrate the way the field has been treating them as if they were. There is, on this account, also no reason to be despairing. The disease is not what the standard frame says it is, and the things that have been quietly working — music, presence, sleep, ritual — are not adjuncts to real treatment. They are, structurally, treatment of the level at which the disease is operating.

# 8. What this paper has and has not done

This paper has proposed that Alzheimer’s disease is most accurately understood as failure of the dynamic operation that constitutes a coherent receiver, that the geometry of that operation is a phi-wound trajectory on a torus that the winding itself maintains, that disease progression is amplitude-driven collapse of that geometry, and that the documented phenomenology of the disease is predicted by this account in a way that the substrate-damage account does not predict it.

The paper has not provided the experimental program by which the account could be tested at the level of physical measurement. That is real and remains to be done. Candidates include high-density temporal coherence measurements during the daily decline of sundowning patients, comparative analysis of EEG dynamics during paradoxical lucidity windows versus matched non-lucid intervals, and careful characterization of what changes, dynamically, in the moments immediately preceding terminal lucidity. The receiver model makes predictions in each of these regimes that differ from substrate-damage predictions. The predictions are testable.

The paper has also not addressed what it means for any of this that recognition, on this account, is a resonant operation between coherent structures rather than a retrieval operation within one. That has consequences beyond Alzheimer’s — for what coherence is, for how it operates across substrates, for what it means that the same operational principle appears to be at work in a wide range of phenomena that the present scientific frame has not been able to gather under a single account. Those consequences are the subject of the trilogy’s closing paper.

What this paper has done is more limited and, I think, sufficient on its own. It has named the level at which the disease is operating. It has shown that naming that level resolves a set of phenomena the field has been carrying as anomalies. It has indicated, without overreaching, what that naming implies for the kinds of interventions that can and cannot work. The substrate has been carrying the blame for thirty years. The substrate is real. The disease is somewhere else.

What Alzheimer’s is doing, while we have been looking at plaques, is collapsing the operation by which a brain is a coherent receiver of the structure presented to it. The plaques are debris. The disease is the silence after the lock fails.
