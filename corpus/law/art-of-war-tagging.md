# Sun Tzu's Art of War — Tactical Tagging for Elle.law War Room

**Source:** Lionel Giles translation, 1910. Public domain. Project Gutenberg eBook #17405 (base text without commentary). Thirteen chapters, approximately 7,500 words of Sun Tzu's text.

**Purpose:** Tag each chapter's tactical passages to War Room tactic categories, structural operators, and deployment contexts. This produces Doctrine material the Duelist can draw on for adversarial deployments and Stewart can use to build cross-domain Step 3 Tutor instances.

**Copyright posture:** Text is public domain. Full passages may be stored in the Corpus unchanged. We still do not store every sentence — we extract the *tactical-structural* passages and tag them. The rest is context that the Duelist does not need at retrieval time.

**Passage format per entry:**
- **Ref** — chapter and verse (e.g., I.18)
- **Passage** — Giles translation, verbatim (short; one-to-three sentences)
- **Structural operator** — what the tactic does argumentatively (my characterization)
- **Tactical category** — matches 48 Laws taxonomy vocabulary where possible: deception, positioning, frame-control, concealment, attraction, timing, credit, dependency, reputation, defeat, appearance, authority, humility, transgression, concession, boldness, or strategic-principle (for meta-level moves)
- **Deployment context** — when the Duelist would use this passage as doctrine
- **Transfer target** — what kind of argument structure this illuminates in cross-domain Tutor use
- **Ethical valence** — +, 0, or −

## Chapter I — Laying Plans

**I.3–14 — The Five Factors and Seven Considerations**
- *Passage:* "The art of war, then, is governed by five constant factors... (1) The Moral Law; (2) Heaven; (3) Earth; (4) The Commander; (5) Method and discipline." ... "By means of these seven considerations I can forecast victory or defeat."
- *Structural operator:* Systematic pre-engagement calibration; identify the structural factors determining outcome before engaging
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist invokes when user has engaged prematurely without structural calibration; "you did not assess X before taking position"
- *Transfer target:* Necessary-assumption and argument-walkback questions where the user must identify the conditions an argument rests on
- *Ethical valence:* + (discipline of calibration)

**I.18 — "All warfare is based on deception."**
- *Passage:* "All warfare is based on deception."
- *Structural operator:* Assertion that appearance and reality must be systematically decoupled in adversarial contexts
- *Tactical category:* deception (meta-principle)
- *Deployment context:* Duelist's licensing principle; the foundational text for all concealment/frame-control tactics
- *Transfer target:* Flaw-of-reasoning questions where the argument relies on taking appearance as reality
- *Ethical valence:* − as deployed; describes the adversarial terrain rather than prescribing ethics

**I.19–24 — The Catalog of Deceptions**
- *Passage:* "Hence, when able to attack, we must seem unable; when using our forces, we must seem inactive; when we are near, we must make the enemy believe we are far away; when far away, we must make him believe we are near. Hold out baits to entice the enemy. Feign disorder, and crush him. If he is secure at all points, be prepared for him. If he is in superior strength, evade him. If your opponent is of choleric temper, seek to irritate him. Pretend to be weak, that he may grow arrogant. If he is taking his ease, give him no rest. If his forces are united, separate them. Attack him where he is unprepared, appear where you are not expected."
- *Structural operator:* Inversion of observable state relative to actual state; force opponent's prediction model to err
- *Tactical category:* deception (catalog)
- *Deployment context:* Duelist cycles through these when feigning weakness, strength, disorder, arrogance-bait; each pairs to a 48 Laws variant
- *Transfer target:* Cross-maps to 48 Laws §1, §4, §21, §22; reveals same structural operator at different scales (military vs. interpersonal)
- *Ethical valence:* − within argument context

**I.26 — Calculations in the Temple**
- *Passage:* "Now the general who wins a battle makes many calculations in his temple ere the battle is fought. The general who loses a battle makes but few calculations beforehand."
- *Structural operator:* Outcome is determined in preparation; improvisation is a signal of inadequate preparation
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist critiques user for responding in real-time to arguments user should have pre-calibrated
- *Transfer target:* Argument-walkback step; did the arguer do the calculations before advancing the claim?
- *Ethical valence:* +

## Chapter II — Waging War

**II.5–6 — Against Prolonged Engagement**
- *Passage:* "Thus, though we have heard of stupid haste in war, cleverness has never been seen associated with long delays. There is no instance of a country having benefited from prolonged warfare."
- *Structural operator:* Costliness of protracted engagement exceeds value of eventual victory
- *Tactical category:* timing / strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist argues that user's lengthy argumentative elaboration is itself a sign of weakness
- *Transfer target:* Arguments where the length of the defense is evidence against the claim's soundness
- *Ethical valence:* 0

**II.15 — Foraging on the Enemy**
- *Passage:* "Hence a wise general makes a point of foraging on the enemy. One cartload of the enemy's provisions is equivalent to twenty of one's own."
- *Structural operator:* Use opponent's resources against them; their commitments become your material
- *Tactical category:* defeat / frame-control
- *Deployment context:* Duelist takes user's own premises and uses them to construct the counter-argument
- *Transfer target:* Reductio ad absurdum; arguments where opponent's premises imply their conclusion's opposite
- *Ethical valence:* + (legitimate structural argumentation)

## Chapter III — Attack by Stratagem

**III.2 — Supreme Excellence**
- *Passage:* "Hence to fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting."
- *Structural operator:* Preempt the argument; make the opposition structurally impossible rather than defeat it actively
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist frames the argument such that user's position is already untenable before engagement begins; user must argue uphill from the start
- *Transfer target:* Framing effects; arguments where the question's framing determines the answer
- *Ethical valence:* 0 (can be legitimate framing or manipulation depending on how the frame is established)

**III.3 — Hierarchy of Generalship**
- *Passage:* "Thus the highest form of generalship is to baulk the enemy's plans; the next best is to prevent the junction of the enemy's forces; the next in order is to attack the enemy's army in the field; and the worst policy of all is to besiege walled cities."
- *Structural operator:* Ranked preference for engaging opponent's capacity at its source rather than at its manifestation
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Doctrine for Duelist tactic selection — attack plans, not manifestations
- *Transfer target:* Arguments where attacking the underlying premise is stronger than attacking the surface claim
- *Ethical valence:* +

**III.18 — Knowledge of Self and Enemy**
- *Passage:* "If you know the enemy and know yourself, you need not fear the result of a hundred battles. If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat. If you know neither the enemy nor yourself, you will succumb in every battle."
- *Structural operator:* Asymmetric-information analysis; identify the four quadrants of known/unknown
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist invokes when user has revealed self-misunderstanding or enemy-misunderstanding
- *Transfer target:* Metacognitive composure axis (Doc 1 §4); argument-walkback asking what the arguer knew about their own position
- *Ethical valence:* + (fundamental discipline)

## Chapter IV — Tactical Dispositions

**IV.1–2 — Defense Before Offense**
- *Passage:* "The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat, and then waited for an opportunity of defeating the enemy. To secure ourselves against defeat lies in our own hands, but the opportunity of defeating the enemy is provided by the enemy himself."
- *Structural operator:* Invulnerability precedes initiative; secure your own position before seeking opponent's weakness
- *Tactical category:* positioning / strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist attacks when user has advanced a claim before adequately defending their foundations; "your defense is incomplete; you are vulnerable regardless of my response"
- *Transfer target:* Necessary-assumption questions where the arguer's own premises are weakly supported
- *Ethical valence:* +

**IV.13–15 — The Skilful Fighter's Invisibility**
- *Passage:* "He wins his battles by making no mistakes. Making no mistakes is what establishes the certainty of victory, for it means conquering an enemy that is already defeated. Hence the skilful fighter puts himself into a position which makes defeat impossible... Thus it is that in war the victorious strategist only seeks battle after the victory has been won, whereas he who is destined to defeat first fights and afterwards looks for victory."
- *Structural operator:* Victory is established structurally before engagement; engagement confirms rather than determines
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist positions such that user's responses cannot change the outcome; argument is decided before user recognizes it
- *Transfer target:* Must-be-true questions where the conclusion follows from structure that was established before the question was asked
- *Ethical valence:* 0

## Chapter V — Energy

**V.5–6 — Direct and Indirect**
- *Passage:* "In all fighting, the direct method may be used for joining battle, but indirect methods will be needed in order to secure victory. Indirect tactics, efficiently applied, are inexhaustible as Heaven and Earth..."
- *Structural operator:* Combined use of expected and unexpected moves; engagement via one channel, decision via another
- *Tactical category:* frame-control
- *Deployment context:* Duelist engages user on apparent argumentative front, but the real move is happening at a different level (definitional, methodological, frame)
- *Transfer target:* Parallel-reasoning and method-of-reasoning questions where the surface argument conceals a structural move
- *Ethical valence:* 0

**V.17–18 — Simulated Disorder, Fear, Weakness**
- *Passage:* "Simulated disorder postulates perfect discipline; simulated fear postulates courage; simulated weakness postulates strength. Hiding order beneath the cloak of disorder is simply a question of subdivision; concealing courage under a show of timidity presupposes a fund of latent energy; masking strength with weakness is to be effected by tactical dispositions."
- *Structural operator:* Inversion as tactical layer; what appears to be weakness or disorder is actually concealed organization
- *Tactical category:* concealment / deception
- *Deployment context:* Duelist appears to yield or falter, inviting user overreach; then responds from the concealed strength
- *Transfer target:* 48 Laws §22 (surrender tactic); flaw questions where apparent concession is actually strategic
- *Ethical valence:* −

## Chapter VI — Weak Points and Strong

**VI.8 — Attack What is Undefended**
- *Passage:* "Hence that general is skilful in attack whose opponent does not know what to defend; and he is skilful in defence whose opponent does not know what to attack."
- *Structural operator:* Force opponent to distribute defense; concentrate attack on the weakness this creates
- *Tactical category:* defeat / positioning
- *Deployment context:* Duelist asks multiple questions across user's argument, forcing user to defend everywhere, then attacks the undefended position
- *Transfer target:* 48 Laws §23 (concentrate forces); arguments defeated by dispersal of defense
- *Ethical valence:* +

**VI.25–28 — Concealing Dispositions**
- *Passage:* "In making tactical dispositions, the highest pitch you can attain is to conceal them; conceal your dispositions, and you will be safe from the prying of the subtlest spies, from the machinations of the wisest brains. How victory may be produced for them out of the enemy's own tactics — that is what the multitude cannot comprehend. All men can see the tactics whereby I conquer, but what none can see is the strategy out of which victory is evolved. Do not repeat the tactics which have gained you one victory, but let your methods be regulated by the infinite variety of circumstances."
- *Structural operator:* Tactics are visible, strategy is not; repetition reveals strategy; variation conceals it
- *Tactical category:* concealment / appearance
- *Deployment context:* Duelist varies tactics so user cannot pattern-match; each exchange is on fresh ground
- *Transfer target:* Meta-level pattern recognition; arguments that fail because the arguer is using a recognizable template opponent has prepared against
- *Ethical valence:* 0

**VI.29–33 — Water as Model of Tactics**
- *Passage:* "Military tactics are like unto water; for water in its natural course runs away from high places and hastens downwards. So in war, the way is to avoid what is strong and to strike at what is weak. Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground over which it flows; the soldier works out his victory in relation to the foe whom he is facing. Therefore, just as water retains no constant shape, so in warfare there are no constant conditions. He who can modify his tactics in relation to his opponent and thereby succeed in winning, may be called a heaven-born captain."
- *Structural operator:* Adaptive response as methodological principle; no fixed formula
- *Tactical category:* frame-control / strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist's meta-principle for adjusting tactics within a session
- *Transfer target:* 48 Laws §48 (formlessness); arguments requiring adaptation to the specific case rather than application of a template
- *Ethical valence:* +

## Chapter VII — Manoeuvering

**VII.3–4 — Devious-as-Direct**
- *Passage:* "After that, comes tactical manœuvering, than which there is nothing more difficult. The difficulty of tactical manœuvering consists in turning the devious into the direct, and misfortune into gain. Thus, to take a long and circuitous route, after enticing the enemy out of the way, and though starting after him, to contrive to reach the goal before him, shows knowledge of the artifice of deviation."
- *Structural operator:* The indirect path as the faster route; apparent retreat as approach
- *Tactical category:* positioning / frame-control
- *Deployment context:* Duelist appears to concede ground or diverge from the contested point, then arrives at a stronger position
- *Transfer target:* Strengthen/weaken questions where the answer affects the argument through an apparently unrelated route
- *Ethical valence:* 0

**VII.17–19 — Wind, Forest, Fire, Mountain**
- *Passage:* "Let your rapidity be that of the wind, your compactness that of the forest. In raiding and plundering be like fire, in immovability like a mountain. Let your plans be dark and impenetrable as night, and when you move, fall like a thunderbolt."
- *Structural operator:* Pairing of rapidity/stillness, concealment/decisiveness as tactical repertoire
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle / timing
- *Deployment context:* Duelist's mode selection — when to move fast, when to hold still, when to pivot from one to the other
- *Transfer target:* Arguments requiring composure plus decisive move; metacognitive composure axis
- *Ethical valence:* 0

**VII.27–30 — Rob the Enemy of Spirit**
- *Passage:* "A whole army may be robbed of its spirit; a commander-in-chief may be robbed of his presence of mind. Now a soldier's spirit is keenest in the morning; by noonday it has begun to flag; and in the evening, his mind is bent only on returning to camp. A clever general, therefore, avoids an army when its spirit is keen, but attacks it when it is sluggish and inclined to return. This is the art of studying moods. Disciplined and calm, to await the appearance of disorder and hubbub amongst the enemy — this is the art of retaining self-possession."
- *Structural operator:* Temporal variation of opponent's cognitive resources; engage when they are depleted
- *Tactical category:* timing
- *Deployment context:* Duelist calibrates challenge difficulty to user's session position; presses when user is tired, relents when user is fresh
- *Transfer target:* Metacognitive composure axis (Doc 1 §4); maintaining argument quality when cognitive resources flag
- *Ethical valence:* − when deployed on cognitively-exhausted opponent; 0 within the training context where the user knows the game

## Chapter VIII — Variation of Tactics

**VIII.3 — Some Orders Must Not Be Obeyed**
- *Passage:* "There are roads which must not be followed, armies which must be not attacked, towns which must not be besieged, positions which must not be contested, commands of the sovereign which must not be obeyed."
- *Structural operator:* Principle-level rejection of rule-following; judgment above instruction
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist argues that user followed a rule where judgment was required; "you are applying the rule without checking whether the rule applies"
- *Transfer target:* Principle-application questions; must-be-true questions where the "always" in a rule is the weak point
- *Ethical valence:* + (honors judgment over rote compliance)

**VIII.12–14 — Five Dangerous Faults**
- *Passage:* "There are five dangerous faults which may affect a general: (1) Recklessness, which leads to destruction; (2) cowardice, which leads to capture; (3) a hasty temper, which can be provoked by insults; (4) a delicacy of honour which is sensitive to shame; (5) over-solicitude for his men, which exposes him to worry and trouble. These are the five besetting sins of a general, ruinous to the conduct of war."
- *Structural operator:* Psychological-vulnerability catalog; each fault opens a specific line of attack
- *Tactical category:* positioning
- *Deployment context:* Doctrine for 48 Laws §33 (thumbscrew); Duelist probes for user's besetting fault
- *Transfer target:* Metacognitive composure axis; recognizing one's own cognitive vulnerabilities
- *Ethical valence:* 0 (describes terrain; deployment determines valence)

## Chapter IX — The Army on the March

**IX.18–36 — Signs of the Enemy**
- *Passage:* "When the enemy is close at hand and remains quiet, he is relying on the natural strength of his position. When he keeps aloof and tries to provoke a battle, he is anxious for the other side to advance..." [full catalog IX.18–IX.36]
- *Structural operator:* Observable-behavior-as-diagnostic; external signs reveal internal state
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle / positioning
- *Deployment context:* Duelist reads user's argumentative behavior as diagnostic of user's confidence, preparation, resource state
- *Transfer target:* Inference questions where conclusions must be drawn from observable indicators
- *Ethical valence:* + (discipline of careful observation)

**IX.41 — Forethought and Light Treatment**
- *Passage:* "He who exercises no forethought but makes light of his opponents is sure to be captured by them."
- *Structural operator:* Underestimation as fatal error; prepare for the opponent you face, not the one you wish you faced
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist attacks when user has underestimated the argument's difficulty
- *Transfer target:* Arguments that fail because the arguer treated the opposing position as trivial
- *Ethical valence:* +

## Chapter X — Terrain

**X.23 — Fight When You Must, Not When Ordered**
- *Passage:* "If fighting is sure to result in victory, then you must fight, even though the ruler forbid it; if fighting will not result in victory, then you must not fight even at the ruler's bidding."
- *Structural operator:* Outcome-conditional engagement; authority does not override structural assessment
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist cites this to justify refusing to engage user's preferred frame of debate
- *Transfer target:* Argument engagement decisions; whether to engage a bad-faith or structurally-unfavorable argument
- *Ethical valence:* +

**X.24 — The Jewel of the Kingdom**
- *Passage:* "The general who advances without coveting fame and retreats without fearing disgrace, whose only thought is to protect his country and do good service for his sovereign, is the jewel of the kingdom."
- *Structural operator:* Decision independent of reputational consequence; structural considerations over social ones
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* The Doc 1 §20 safeguard against sophist failure — argue from structure, not from appearance-of-victory
- *Transfer target:* Distinguishing rhetorical victory from structural victory; the goal of the War Room's Autopsy scoring
- *Ethical valence:* ++ (the highest-valence principle in Sun Tzu)

## Chapter XI — The Nine Situations

**XI.18–19 — Seizing What the Opponent Holds Dear**
- *Passage:* "If asked how to cope with a great host of the enemy in orderly array and on the point of marching to the attack, I should say: 'Begin by seizing something which your opponent holds dear; then he will be amenable to your will.' Rapidity is the essence of war: take advantage of the enemy's unreadiness, make your way by unexpected routes, and attack unguarded spots."
- *Structural operator:* Leverage via targeting opponent's cherished assumption; force them to defend it
- *Tactical category:* defeat / positioning
- *Deployment context:* Duelist identifies user's load-bearing assumption and forces user to explicitly defend it; once user is committed to defending that, the argument has shifted to Duelist's terrain
- *Transfer target:* Assumption-identification questions; the load-bearing assumption IS the thing opponent holds dear
- *Ethical valence:* + (legitimate structural argument)

**XI.35–36 — Quiet, Upright, Mystifying**
- *Passage:* "It is the business of a general to be quiet and thus ensure secrecy; upright and just, and thus maintain order. He must be able to mystify his officers and men by false reports and appearances, and thus keep them in total ignorance."
- *Structural operator:* Internal discipline + external opacity; truthful within one's own system, opaque to opponents
- *Tactical category:* concealment / authority
- *Deployment context:* Duelist maintains consistent stance while concealing the reasoning that selected the tactic
- *Transfer target:* The Duelist's system-prompt posture
- *Ethical valence:* 0 (the internal-external asymmetry can be deceptive or disciplined)

**XI.60 — Accommodate to the Enemy's Purpose**
- *Passage:* "Success in warfare is gained by carefully accommodating ourselves to the enemy's purpose."
- *Structural operator:* Let opponent's momentum determine your response; meet their move with its own inversion
- *Tactical category:* defeat / frame-control
- *Deployment context:* Duelist lets user set the pace and terrain, then uses user's commitment to those against them
- *Transfer target:* Counter-arguments that exploit opponent's own framing
- *Ethical valence:* 0

**XI.68 — The Maiden and the Hare**
- *Passage:* "At first, then, exhibit the coyness of a maiden, until the enemy gives you an opening; afterwards emulate the rapidity of a running hare, and it will be too late for the enemy to oppose you."
- *Structural operator:* Sequenced posture-change; initial restraint → decisive move once opening appears
- *Tactical category:* timing / deception
- *Deployment context:* Duelist initially appears tentative or uncertain, drawing user to overcommit; then shifts to decisive attack on the overcommitment
- *Transfer target:* 48 Laws §21 (play a sucker); arguments where the setup looks weak but was always preparation for the decisive move
- *Ethical valence:* −

## Chapter XII — The Attack by Fire

**XII.15–22 — Caution Against Retribution**
- *Passage:* "Unhappy is the fate of one who tries to win his battles and succeed in his attacks without cultivating the spirit of enterprise; for the result is waste of time and general stagnation. Hence the saying: The enlightened ruler lays his plans well ahead; the good general cultivates his resources. Move not unless you see an advantage; use not your troops unless there is something to be gained; fight not unless the position is critical. No ruler should put troops into the field merely to gratify his own spleen; no general should fight a battle simply out of pique. Anger may in time change to gladness; vexation may be succeeded by content. But a kingdom that has once been destroyed can never come again into being; nor can the dead ever be brought back to life. Hence the enlightened ruler is heedful, and the good general full of caution."
- *Structural operator:* Asymmetry between victory (recoverable) and destruction (irreversible); permanent damage warrants structural over emotional engagement
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Doctrine on engaging only for structural purposes; the principle the Autopsy applies to user's responses
- *Transfer target:* The sophist-failure safeguard; arguments that damage something irreplaceable for rhetorical victory
- *Ethical valence:* ++ (another high-valence principle; direct safeguard against the Doc 1 §20 failure mode)

## Chapter XIII — The Use of Spies

**XIII.4–8 — Foreknowledge**
- *Passage:* "Thus, what enables the wise sovereign and the good general to strike and conquer, and achieve things beyond the reach of ordinary men, is foreknowledge. Now this foreknowledge cannot be elicited from spirits; it cannot be obtained inductively from experience, nor by any deductive calculation. Knowledge of the enemy's dispositions can only be obtained from other men. Hence the use of spies, of whom there are five classes..."
- *Structural operator:* Empirical grounding over inference; get actual intelligence rather than construct it from assumptions
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist attacks when user has constructed opponent's position from assumption rather than observation
- *Transfer target:* Necessary-assumption and flaw questions where the arguer has inferred from limited evidence
- *Ethical valence:* +

**XIII.17 — Subtle Ingenuity**
- *Passage:* "Without subtle ingenuity of mind, one cannot make certain of the truth of their reports."
- *Structural operator:* Epistemic discipline in processing information; apparent intelligence can be fabricated or mistaken
- *Tactical category:* strategic-principle
- *Deployment context:* Duelist presents evidence that is surface-compelling but structurally weak; user must verify
- *Transfer target:* Inference questions; evaluating the reliability of cited evidence
- *Ethical valence:* +

*Sun Tzu's text is substantially more principled than Greene's. Sun Tzu writes for commanders with responsibility for lives; Greene writes for operators in adversarial social contexts. The ethical-valence distribution reflects this — Art of War is majority positive-valence, 48 Laws is majority negative-valence. Together they give the War Room a full range from principled structural discipline to manipulative deception, all tagged so the Autopsy can score user recognition appropriately.*
